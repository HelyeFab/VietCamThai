# DAY BY DAY — Bangkok

## Day 1 — October 1, 2026 — Welcome to the City of Angels

![Wat Arun — the Temple of Dawn](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Templo_Wat_Arun%2C_Bangkok%2C_Tailandia%2C_2013-08-22%2C_DD_04.jpg/1280px-Templo_Wat_Arun%2C_Bangkok%2C_Tailandia%2C_2013-08-22%2C_DD_04.jpg)

### Arrival (18:10)

Landing in Bangkok at **18:10** on 1 October, today is mostly about the transfer and a first taste of the city, with no demanding sightseeing — the Grand Palace and the great temples are tomorrow's business. This evening is for the formalities and settling into your neighbourhood, your base for the next four nights.

**On arrival, in order:**
- **Immigration**: have your passport ready and (if required) the Thailand Digital Arrival Card already filled in online — evening queues can be long
- **Baggage claim** at the carousels, then customs check (usually the green channel, nothing to declare)
- **SIM/eSIM**: stop at the AIS/TrueMove counters in the arrivals hall (open 24h) for a tourist SIM, or activate an eSIM you bought before the flight
- **Cash**: withdraw from an airport ATM (fee ~220 THB) or change a first stash — many stalls and taxis only accept cash
- **Hotel transfer**: you have a **private transfer already booked** waiting in the arrivals area — look for the driver with the sign and he'll take you straight to The Cotton Saladaeng, no queues or haggling (~45-60 min in evening traffic). *(Plan B if things go wrong: Grab from the Public Transport counter, ~350-450 THB / €10-13.)*

**Your hotel (base for all 4 nights):**

**The Cotton Saladaeng Hotel** — 14, 1 Soi Sathon 2, Si Lom, Bang Rak. You're in the heart of the **Silom/Sathon** district, quiet and superbly connected: a short walk from **BTS Sala Daeng** station and **MRT Silom**, with Lumphini Park right nearby. From here you'll easily reach the temples, Chinatown and the markets on the following days.

**Getting around from here (your base for the whole stay):** right below the hotel, the two networks you'll use every day cross —
- **BTS Silom Line** (**Sala Daeng** station): towards Saphan Taksin/Sathorn Pier (for the temples by river) and towards Siam (for Jim Thompson, shopping and the skyscrapers)
- **MRT Blue Line** (**Si Lom** station): direct to **Wat Mangkon** (Chinatown) with no changes
- **Grab**: cheap, no haggling, ideal in the evening and for routes not covered by the trains

Get yourself a **Rabbit Card** for the BTS (rechargeable) and use a ticket/QR for the MRT; keep some cash for Grab and street food. With this base you'll almost always avoid the road traffic.

### Evening

After check-in, take a first, unhurried taste of Bangkok close to the hotel:
- **A stroll through Silom/Sathon**: the little lanes (soi) around Sala Daeng fill with street food stalls after sunset — a gentle way to get your first feel for the city
- **First dinner nearby**: pad thai, pad krapow moo (pork with holy basil) or a rice dish; the area is packed with restaurants, and lively Silom Soi 4 is close by
- An ice-cold **Chang or Singha** to toast the start of the trip

> If you land tired, a light dinner and an early night are absolutely fine — there's no sightseeing tonight, and tomorrow you set off fresh and early for the Grand Palace.

### Tips of the Day

- **SIM/eSIM**: sort it at the airport — the staff handle passport registration and activation in a few minutes; if the queues are long, there's free airport WiFi to call a Grab
- **Your driver is waiting**: after customs look for the sign with your name in the arrivals area; if you don't find it right away, contact the transfer number provided by the hotel/agency before wandering off
- **Address ready**: keep "The Cotton Saladaeng Hotel, Soi Sathon 2" saved in Thai on your phone — handy for Grabs on the following days
- **Early start tomorrow**: the Grand Palace opens at 8:30 — it's worth being there at opening to avoid the crowds and the heat

## Day 2 — October 2, 2026 — Great Temples and the River of Kings

### Morning

**How to get there from the hotel (by river, the loveliest way):** from **BTS Sala Daeng** take the Silom Line to the terminus, **Saphan Taksin** (~15 min), and leave by exit 2 for **Sathorn Pier / Central Pier**. Board the **Chao Phraya Express Boat** (tourist boat, orange/blue flag) and get off at **Tha Chang (N9)**, a short walk from the Grand Palace. Buy the **Tourist Boat Pass** this morning (150 THB, unlimited hop-on hop-off until 19:15) — you'll want it again in the afternoon and evening for Wat Arun. Alternatively a direct Grab (~150-200 THB), but in morning traffic the boat is often faster — and infinitely more scenic.

**Grand Palace & Wat Phra Kaew (Temple of the Emerald Buddha)** — arrive at opening (8:30) to beat the crowds and the heat
- **Hours**: 8:30-15:30 every day
- **Price**: 500 THB per person (~€14), children under 120cm free
- **Included**: also access to Wat Pho (within 7 days), Vimanmek Mansion and Royal Thai Decorations
- **Official site**: [royalgrandpalace.th](https://www.royalgrandpalace.th/en/)

STRICT dress code: shoulders and knees covered, closed shoes (leggings NOT accepted). Free sarongs at the entrance if you're not appropriately dressed.

**Don't miss**: the Temple of the Emerald Buddha (Phra Kaew Morakot), carved from a single block of green jade, and the Chakri Maha Prasat Hall, a Victorian palace with Thai roofs.

⚠️ **Scam alert**: the tuk-tuks near the Grand Palace sometimes claim it's "closed for a Buddhist holiday" to divert you to gem shops — always go to the gate (West Gate, Na Phra Lan Road) to check. No online booking: tickets are only sold at the gate.

**Wat Pho (Reclining Buddha)** — 650m from the Grand Palace, an easy walk
- **Hours**: 8:30-18:30, 100 THB
- **Highlight**: the 46-metre gold-plated reclining Buddha, a symbol of spiritual enlightenment; Wat Pho is also the birthplace of traditional Thai massage, if you want to treat yourself to a break

### Afternoon

**Lunch along the river**: stop at one of the little eateries in Tha Tien, or buy something from the vendors — the Chao Phraya boats sell snacks and drinks for a few baht.

**The Chao Phraya, the water road of kings**: the river is the loveliest (and coolest) way to get around this area.
- **Tourist Boat Pass**: 150 THB (~€4) to hop on and off all day between 8:30 and 19:15
- **Single ticket**: 40 THB for one ride

The early-afternoon heat is the cue to slow down: a break in the shade, a Thai massage, or a quiet boat ride while you wait for the light to soften towards sunset.

### Evening

**Wat Arun (Temple of Dawn) at sunset** — the grand finale of the day. From **Tha Tien (N8)** pier, next to Wat Pho, a short ferry crosses the river in a few minutes.
- **Cross-river ferry**: from Tha Tien Pier (N8) to Wat Arun Pier, 5 THB, every 5-10 minutes, running 05:00-22:00
- **Temple entry**: 200 THB (~€5.50)

Wat Arun is one of Bangkok's most recognisable landmarks, its 70m central tower (prang) built in Khmer style and encrusted with mosaics of glass and porcelain. Climb the steep steps for a view over the river, then — despite the name "Temple of Dawn" — save yourself for the best moment: **at sunset** the tower glows pink and gold against the sky, and as you cross back over the river you'll catch it lit up from the far bank.

**Dinner and return**: dine along the river (the terraces at Tha Tien look straight onto the floodlit Wat Arun) or head back to your base. From the pier, a Grab or the MRT will carry you comfortably back to **Silom/Sala Daeng** and The Cotton Saladaeng, rounding off a full day.

### Tips of the Day

- **Dress code** for the temples: shoulders and knees covered, closed shoes. Leggings NOT accepted at the Grand Palace. Sarongs available at the entrance
- **One ticket, two temples**: entry to the Grand Palace includes access to Wat Pho within 7 days — keep the stub
- **On the boat**: go down to the lower deck as you approach your stop, the boat doesn't wait long
- **Wat Arun timing**: arrive about an hour before sunset to climb the tower while the light is still good and then witness the change of colours

## Day 3 — October 3, 2026 — Modern Bangkok & Chinatown

After all those grand temples, today shifts gears: the modern Bangkok of skyscrapers and malls in the morning, the chaotic, Chinese Bangkok of Yaowarat by night. A more relaxed day, with some free time in between.

### Morning

**Jim Thompson House** — the most elegant oasis in the modern city
- **Hours**: 10:00-18:00 every day (last guided tour at 17:00)
- **Price**: 200 THB (~€5.50), reduced for students
- **How to get there**: BTS to National Stadium, then a 5-minute walk; from your base the BTS from Sala Daeng with a change at Siam is very convenient

Six traditional Thai teak houses, set in a tropical garden beside the canal, assembled by the American Jim Thompson — the man who revived the Thai silk industry in the 1950s before vanishing without trace in 1967. The guided tour (included, in English) tells the story of his art collection and of silk itself.

**Siam & CentralWorld** — a few BTS stops away
- A stroll through Bangkok's commercial heart: **Siam Paragon**, **Siam Center**, **Siam Discovery** and the huge **CentralWorld**
- Optional shopping (from high-tech to local brands) or simply a wander in the air conditioning during the hottest hours
- For cheap buys, the nearby **MBK Center** is still a classic

### Afternoon

**Lunch**: the food courts in the Siam malls are excellent, clean and reasonably priced (dishes 60-120 THB) — try the prepaid-card system.

**Free time**: the afternoon is yours — rest at the hotel, head back to explore Silom, treat yourself to a Thai massage or carry on shopping. Recharge, because the evening belongs to Chinatown.

### Evening

**Yaowarat (Chinatown) — kingdom of street food**
- **When**: the area bursts into life after 18:00, as the neon signs flicker on and the stalls take over the pavements
- **How to get there**: very convenient from your base — the **MRT Blue Line from Si Lom** takes you straight to **Wat Mangkon** (~15 min, no changes), and you come out right in the heart of Yaowarat

**To try among the stalls:**
- **Guy Kao Grilled Squid** — grilled squid with spicy sauce
- **Yaowarat Toasted Buns** — toasted buns with sweet fillings, recognisable by the yellow banner
- **Nai Pon Kao Seer Po** — among the best street food in the area according to the locals
- Skewers, noodles, soups, fruit and sweets — eat a little at each stall and you'll taste a lot

**After dinner (optional) — a night market:**
- 🥇 **Jodd Fairs** (first choice): a modern, lively night market near MRT Phra Ram 9, trendy food stalls, the famous rib "volcano" (Leng Saap) and a young atmosphere
- 🥈 **Srinagarindra Train Night Market (Talad Rot Fai Srinakarin)**: if the group prefers something bigger and more traditional, an enormous vintage market with antiques, classic cars, bars and street food (open Thu-Sun, more out of the way to the east)

From either one a Grab takes you back to The Cotton Saladaeng in ~20-30 min.

### Tips of the Day

- **Chinatown on an empty stomach**: arrive hungry and carry cash — the stalls don't take cards, and prices are rock-bottom but cash only
- **One market per evening**: Jodd Fairs is more convenient and central, Talad Rot Fai Srinakarin is bigger but takes more travel time — pick just one
- **Stay hydrated**: it's hot and humid, alternate water and cha yen (Thai iced tea) between tastings
- **Comfy shoes**: Yaowarat is best experienced by walking slowly through the crowd

## Day 4 — October 4, 2026 — Among Rails and Canals

Your last day in Bangkok is a day trip to the two most photogenic markets in Thailand: the **Maeklong railway market**, where the stalls fold away and reopen as the train passes, and the **Amphawa floating market**, where you dine from boats on the canal. A very early start, and back to the Silom base in the evening.

### Morning (EARLY start!)

**Maeklong Railway Market (Talad Rom Hoop)** — "the market that folds up"
- **Distance**: ~75 km southwest of Bangkok (1-1.5h)
- **How to get there — recommended for a group of 4**: hire a **private van/car with driver for the whole day**, with **pickup directly at The Cotton Saladaeng** (~2,000-2,500 THB total, i.e. **~€15-18 each**). Set off at whatever time you like, the driver knows the train times and takes you from Maeklong to Amphawa and back with no worries — the most sensible option since the public minivans leave from terminals far from Silom
- **Alternatives**: an **organised tour** of Maeklong + Amphawa with hotel pickup (convenient, similar price); or the **public minivans** from Mo Chit or the Southern Bus Terminal / Sai Tai Mai (~80-100 THB / €3 per person, cheap but you have to reach the terminal at dawn by Grab/BTS first)
- **Entry**: free

⚠️ **It's all in the timing**: the market is built literally **on the tracks** of a still-working railway. Eight times a day the train inches through at walking pace, and moments before it arrives the vendors whip back their awnings and baskets to let it past — then slide them out again the instant it's gone. This is the famous "folding umbrellas" spectacle (hence the nickname *Talad Rom Hoop*). **Time your visit to catch a train**: the trains come through at roughly **08:30, 11:10, 14:30 and 17:40** — check the times on the spot or with your driver/tour, as they can shift.

**Pro tip**: grab a seat at one of the little bars along the tracks about ten minutes before the train is due, keep your hands (and feet) inside the yellow line, and enjoy the moment when the market "folds up" and then springs back to life as the train rolls through.

### Afternoon

**Lunch**: among the Maeklong stalls or at a riverside eatery nearby — very fresh fish and seafood, noodles, tropical fruit.

**Amphawa Floating Market** — the floating market, ~15 km from Maeklong
- **Hours**: mainly **Friday, Saturday and Sunday**, from early afternoon until evening (~11:00-20:00)
- **Atmosphere**: the best moment is **late afternoon**, when the light softens, the stalls light up and you dine on street food cooked right on the boats moored along the canal
- **Entry**: free (you only pay for food, drinks and boat rides)

More authentic and local than the touristy floating markets, Amphawa is a tangle of canals, wooden houses and sampans laden with fried mussels, grilled prawns, noodles, coffee and sweets. A **boat ride** (~50 THB per person) carries you along the river past the temples, and towards evening some tours push on to the stretches where, once it's dark, you can watch **fireflies** twinkling in the trees along the water.

### Evening

**Return to Bangkok** (1-1.5h) and back to your base in **Silom/Sala Daeng**: with the private van they drop you right below the hotel.

**Last Bangkok evening**: after a full-on day, take the neighbourhood at a gentle pace — a quiet dinner in one of the soi around The Cotton Saladaeng, perhaps a last Thai massage or a rooftop bar for a farewell toast to the City of Angels. Pack your bags at your leisure and get some rest: Cambodia awaits tomorrow.

### Tips of the Day

- **Departure at dawn**: to catch the first train at ~08:30 it's best to leave Bangkok around 6:30-7:00; if you'd rather sleep in a little, aim for a later train and flip the day around (though Amphawa first in the afternoon doesn't quite work — Maeklong is only worth it with the train)
- **Weekend for Amphawa**: the floating market is at its best Fri-Sun; 4 October 2026 is a **Sunday**, so it's perfect
- **Private van for 4**: split between four, the private car with driver costs almost the same as the public minivans but picks you up at the hotel, handles the train timing and doesn't tie you to fixed schedules — the most convenient choice for this trip
- **Cash and comfy shoes**: both markets are cash only; bring water, a hat and shoes that dry quickly (the Amphawa jetties can be wet)
