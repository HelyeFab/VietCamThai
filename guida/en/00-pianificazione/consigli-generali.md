## General Tips — The Golden Rules of Travel

### Before You Leave

**Strategic bookings**

Book accommodation on Booking.com or Hostelworld with free cancellation for the first few days, and stay flexible for the rest of the trip. For Angkor Wat, buy your passes in advance and think about hiring a guide to bring the history to life.

⚡ **INSIDER TRICK**: Only lock in your bookings for the last 5 days — until then, keep everything changeable!

**Essential checklist**
- Visas: Thailand grants EU citizens 60 days visa-free, but since June 2025 you also need the ETA (Electronic Travel Authorization)
- Travel insurance is mandatory — we recommend Safety Wing for its rolling monthly payments and no lock-in contract
- Credit cards with no foreign fees
- Local eSIMs (Airalo or Nomad) or physical SIM cards to keep you connected

### On the Ground

**How to haggle (when to, when not to)**
- **YES**: Local markets, meterless tuk-tuks, souvenirs
- **NO**: Taxis with a working meter, restaurants with fixed-price menus
- Haggle politely — prices have give in them — and skip hotel referrals to avoid the commission markup


**Avoiding tourist traps**

Sadly, plenty of people will try to take advantage of unsuspecting tourists. Google any service before you use it, and check recent posts on forums like Reddit for scams reported in specific places.

**Common scams to avoid:**
- The "broken" meter, or one that races ahead — always insist on the meter or use Grab
- "Your attraction is closed" — always check the opening hours online or at your hotel reception
- The motorbike damage scam — always inspect the vehicle for existing damage before you accept it
- The "drug deal gone wrong" — steer clear of anything offered to you on the street


### Dress Code for Temples and Sacred Places

**The universal rule**: cover shoulders and knees — simple, but non-negotiable.

**What to bring:**
- A light sarong/pareo, always in your backpack — a lifesaver at the Grand Palace, Angkor Wat and My Son
- A short- or long-sleeved top to cover the shoulders, and a skirt or dress that falls below the knee

**What to avoid:**
- Sleeveless tops, vests (even with a scarf over them), short shorts and leggings at the stricter temples

**Country-specific:**
- **Thailand**: Wat Phra Kaew enforces a very strict code — men in long trousers and a sleeved shirt; women in a skirt or long trousers, no tight leggings
- **Cambodia**: Angkor Wat has no active temples, but the dress code has been strictly enforced since 2016, after a spate of inappropriate behaviour and nude photos



Always take your shoes off before entering, keep calm and quiet, and skip fiddly sandals that are a pain to slip on and off.

### Photography: Rules and Common Sense


Ask before photographing monks or worshippers — not everyone is comfortable being photographed. And above all, no selfies with the Buddha!


Never point your feet at the Buddha — it's deeply disrespectful, as feet are seen as the lowliest part of the body. If you're seated, kneel or tuck your legs to one side.

### Transport

**Essential apps**

In 2026 Grab is Southeast Asia's leading transport app, with 13 new AI features, including a Journey Planner that syncs with your calendar, predicts where you're heading and compares your travel options. It's the region's superapp for rides, food delivery, groceries and more — a clean interface, reliable bookings, real-time tracking and a built-in translator, all serving over 670 million users.

**Traditional vehicles integrated into Grab:**
- **Thailand**: Colourful tuk-tuks and the Rod Daeng (red minibuses) of Chiang Mai, all bookable digitally at transparent prices
- **Vietnam**: GrabBike for weaving through dense streets, and the Cyclo (three-wheeled bicycle taxi) for something more memorable
- **Cambodia**: Tuk-tuks (Remork) — a motorbike towing a carriage — folded in for a more authentic ride


**Night buses: how to survive**
- Bring your own sheets/light sleeping bag
- Extra food and water for delays
- Book transport well ahead during national holidays

### Shopping

**Hoi An Tailors: the tailoring paradise**

Hoi An can turn out high-quality bespoke pieces in a matter of days, for a fraction of what you'd pay almost anywhere else in the world. It's not just the best place in Vietnam to have clothes made — it's known as the tailoring capital of the world.

**Realistic prices 2025-2026:**
- Men's three-piece suit: $90-200 (2-5 million VND); women's short dress: $25-50, long: $40-150
- Standard two-piece suit: $120-250, depending on fabric and construction
- The days of the "good" $50 suit are gone, but for $350-600 you can walk away with a garment to rival the tailoring houses of London or New York


**Process and timing:**
- Allow at least 3 days to shop around, take measurements, fit and collect the finished piece. The first version, based on your initial measurements, takes 24-48 hours
- Most tailors do 1-3 fittings, each 12-24 hours apart


**⚡ INSIDER TRICK**: The tailoring here is dirt cheap — literally 80-95% less than bespoke garments elsewhere — but "you get what you pay for" still holds. A tailor selling made-to-measure for $5-10 almost certainly isn't using decent cloth.

**Tips for success:**
- Before you walk in, have a clear idea of what you want: the type of garment, a basic design, a rough colour. Bringing 1-3 reference images makes it far easier to get your point across
- Always agree the price up front, before you hand over any deposit. Expect to pay 50% down, with the balance on delivery


### Currency Exchange & Payments


GrabPay for Travel is a new payment method that accepts the region's national QR codes and works with the debit/credit cards linked to your Grab account. It saves you fiddling with other banking apps or foreign e-wallets, and bails you out when a vendor won't take cards.

**Golden rule**: Avoid currency exchanges at travel agencies — always use an official bank ATM or pay by card.

### Lan Ha vs Ha Long Bay

⚡ **INSIDER TRICK**: 
Go for Lan Ha — every bit as beautiful as Ha Long, but far less crowded. A 2-day/1-night boat trip beats a 1-day tour hands down.

### Angkor Wat: The Unmissable Sunrise


Leave your Siem Reap hotel at 5:00 — and bring a torch! Sunrise at Angkor Wat is a life-changing sight, but it does mean an early start.

### General Safety


Violent crime is rare, and tens of millions visit every year without a hitch, which makes Southeast Asia one of the safest places in the world to travel. Most people you meet will be kind, thoughtful and honest.


Lock your valuables away — better still, don't bring much of value in the first place. For whatever you do bring, keep it in a small lockable bag or the in-room safe, or leave it locked up at the hotel/hostel. Back pockets are a magnet for pickpockets.

### Remember


Sometimes, however well you prepare, you'll get scammed anyway — maybe for a few dollars, maybe more. In the end, as long as you're safe, that's what counts. Never put yourself in harm's way to claw back money or dodge a scam!

Southeast Asia will give you unforgettable memories — follow these tips and enjoy every moment of this extraordinary adventure! 🌴✨
