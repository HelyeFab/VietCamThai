## Weather & What to Pack

### Expected Climate (1 - 31 October 2026)

Your trip takes place **entirely in October**, in the **final phase of the monsoon season**. But beware: travelling from Bangkok up to Hanoi you'll cross **three very different climates in the same month**, and the moment you reach each region matters enormously. Here's the reality, region by region, in the order of your itinerary:

#### **Thailand — Bangkok (1-4 Oct)**


Tail end of the rainy season: rainfall is easing off compared to September (~180-210mm) but it's still hot and humid. Brief, predictable downpours, often in the late afternoon or at night — easy to plan around.

- **Temperatures**: 31-33°C, high humidity

#### **Cambodia — Siem Reap, Koh Rong Samloem, Phnom Penh (5-12 Oct)**


End of the monsoon: lush green landscapes, the moats of Angkor and the Tonlé Sap full of water, fewer crowds. Brief afternoon downpours, usually a few hours rather than whole days.

- **Temperatures**: 24-33°C, high humidity
- **Island (Koh Rong Samloem)**: the sea is still enjoyable, but check the weather for the ferries

#### **Southern Vietnam — Chau Doc & Mekong, Ho Chi Minh City (13-16 Oct)**


Still the wet season, but the south is the mildest part of Vietnam at this time of year: mornings often clear, rain concentrated in short, intense afternoon downpours. The Mekong Delta is in the middle of the high-water season — perfect for the flooded forest of Tra Su.

- **Temperatures**: 24-32°C

#### **⚠️ Central Vietnam — Hoi An, Hué, Phong Nha (17-24 Oct)**


**The most climatically delicate stretch.** For central Vietnam, October-November is the **peak of the rainy season**: heavy rainfall, a **real risk of flooding** (Hoi An's old town floods almost every year between October and early November) and an active storm/typhoon season. In Phong Nha, high water can limit access to the boat-reachable caves.

- **Temperatures**: 23-30°C, very high humidity
- **To do**: check the forecasts day by day, keep flexible plans, bring a raincoat and dry bags

#### **Northern Vietnam — Ninh Binh, Lan Ha Bay, Hanoi (25-31 Oct)**


The grand finale, and the best period: the north enters the **heart of the dry autumn**. Clear skies, low humidity, cool pleasant temperatures, calm seas for the cruise in Lan Ha Bay. Late October is one of the best windows of the year for Hanoi and Halong.

- **Temperatures**: 21-29°C, dry
- **Note**: by late October the golden rice paddies of Ninh Binh are mostly already harvested

### Complete Packing List (31 days for 4 people)

#### **Clothing (Per Person)**

**TOPS (16-18 pieces total)**
- **8-10 t-shirts/vests** in breathable technical fabric (quick-drying)
- **3 light long-sleeved shirts** for temples, air conditioning, evenings
- **2 "smart" shirts** for restaurants/nights out
- **1 light sweatshirt** for flights and night buses
- **2 swimsuits/bikinis** (absolute minimum - you'll want more!)

**BOTTOMS (10-12 pieces total)**  
- **6 pairs of shorts** light and cool (no heavy jeans)
- **2 pairs of long trousers** breathable, for temples and evenings
- **1 pair of technical trousers** for trekking/activities
- **2-3 light dresses/skirts** for her
- **12+ pairs of underwear** (you sweat a ton!)
- **8-10 pairs of thin socks**

#### **Footwear (Max 4 pairs per person)**

1. **Quality flip-flops** - you'll use them every day
2. **Trekking sandals** like Teva/Keen for hikes and water
3. **Breathable trainers** for cities and transport
4. **Waterproof hiking boots** for anyone doing serious trekking

#### **Essential Accessories**

**For temples and local culture:**
- **2 sarongs/pareos** each (cover shoulders/legs at temples, beach towel, bed sheet)
- **Hat with a visor** - MANDATORY, the sun is brutal
- **Light multi-purpose scarf**

**Protection:**
- **Sunglasses** with a strap
- **SPF 50+ sunscreen** - bring it from Italy, the local stuff often has whitening agents
- **Strong mosquito repellent** (30%+ DEET)
- **Ultralight K-way** or poncho

#### **Electronics and Adapters**


**Great news! You DON'T have to carry a bag full of different adapters. Vietnam, Laos and Cambodia share almost identical standards (a mix of Type A and C). Thailand too is largely compatible with the same universal adapter**
.

**What you need:**
- **1-2 good-quality universal adapters** with surge protection
- **20,000+ mAh power bank** - essential, phones drain super fast
- **Multiple USB cables** (bring backups!)
- **Small power strip** with a European plug (genius for hotels with a single socket)
- **Waterproof case** for your phone

**Plug types in your countries:**
- 
**Cambodia**: Type A, C and G plugs. Standard voltage 230V at 50Hz

- 
**Vietnam**: Type A (two flat vertical pins, common in the US/Canada) and Type C (two round pins, common in continental Europe)

- 
**Thailand**: 220V. Sockets generally take two-pin round or flat plugs


#### **Documents and Security**

**Physical documents:**
- Passport (6+ months' validity)
- Printed visas (if required)
- **2 copies** of the passport in separate places
- **Spare passport photos** (for on-the-spot visas)
- International driving permit
- Printed travel insurance

**Digital backups:**
- 
Photocopies of your documents kept separate from the originals. Digital copies on your laptop (backed up with Dropbox or another cloud, plus an email to yourself), a photo of the passport on your phone and a phone backup to the cloud

- **Emergency cash** in USD (small denominations for visas and bribes)

### Smart Packing Tips

#### **Backpack vs Suitcase: The Verdict**


**For Southeast Asia, made up of multiple islands and rough terrain, we recommend backpacks over wheeled luggage**
.

**Ideal setup for 4 people:**
- 
**2 medium/large backpacks** (40-55L) for most of the luggage and **4 small daypacks** for all the valuables. The large bags can be checked on flights and put in the buses' luggage holds, but the small ones should always stay with you!

- 
**Osprey Porter 46L** - proved to be the perfect backpack! It served its purpose in Europe and Southeast Asia


#### **Ideal Weight and Practicality**

- 
**Keep your main backpack under 10 kg. You won't need more, and you'll probably wear less than you think**

- 
**46 litres is definitely the sweet spot for backpack size. When you have a backpack small enough to carry on board, you can check in for flights on your phone, skip the check-in counter and skip weighing the bag**


#### **Things to Buy THERE Instead of Bringing**

**BUY in Asia (cheaper/better):**
- Local cotton clothes (cool and cheap)
- 
**Dry bag**: easily found in Southeast Asia, especially in watery spots like southern Thailand or the Philippines. It can take up a lot of luggage space, so if you don't plan to use it much, better to get it there

- Basic toiletries
- 
**Mosquito repellents**: skip the toxic high-DEET sprays. Instead, ask the locals what they use against mosquitoes and buy their recommended products. They know what works best


**BRING from Italy (hard to find or expensive):**
- 
**Sunscreen**: in some parts of Southeast Asia, sunscreen without whitening agents can be hard to find. When you do find it, it costs as much as at home

- Specific medications
- Spare contact lenses/glasses
- Quality feminine products

#### **Magic Organisation: Packing Cubes**


**If I could give you just one tip for a Southeast Asia packing list, it would be this: use packing cubes. I'll add: use compressible packing cubes**
.

**Recommended setup per person:**
- 1 cube for underwear/socks
- 1 cube for tops/shirts  
- 1 cube for trousers/shorts
- 1 small cube for electronics
- 
**A dry bag can double as a laundry bag**


#### **Insider Tricks for Night Buses and Boats**

- **Max weight 15kg** per person for comfort on transport
- **Backpack with a rain cover** essential
- 
**A light, waterproof, packable jacket (about €20 from Decathlon works perfectly) if you visit during the wet-season months. This single garment has protected me and my backpack countless times from sudden tropical storms, including when I got off a minivan at the 4000 Islands in Laos in pouring rain. The jacket also works as protection for electronics on travel days**

- **Documents in a waterproof case** always with you
- **Emergency food** (bars) for long journeys

---

**Remember:** 
**There isn't that much of a problem travelling in Southeast Asia during the low season, as long as you know what to expect and plan well**
. Your period is perfect for avoiding the crowds and enjoying better prices, with nature at its post-monsoon best! 

The golden rule? 
**"When you're packing to travel, lay out all your clothes and all your money. Then take half the clothes and twice the money."**
 Okay, the money part may not be feasible, but the clothes part certainly is!
