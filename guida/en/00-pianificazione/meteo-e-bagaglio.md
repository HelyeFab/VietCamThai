### Expected Climate (1 - 31 October 2026)

Your trip falls **entirely in October**, at the **tail end of the monsoon season**. But be warned: travelling from Bangkok up to Hanoi, you'll pass through **three very different climates in a single month**, and exactly when you reach each region matters enormously. Here's how it really looks, region by region, in the order of your itinerary:

#### **Thailand — Bangkok (1-4 Oct)**


The tail end of the rainy season: the rain is easing off from September's peak (~180-210mm), but it's still hot and humid. Brief, predictable downpours, usually in the late afternoon or at night — easy to plan around.

- **Temperatures**: 31-33°C, high humidity

#### **Cambodia — Siem Reap, Koh Rong Samloem, Phnom Penh (5-12 Oct)**


The end of the monsoon: lush green landscapes, the moats of Angkor and the Tonlé Sap brimming with water, and fewer crowds. Brief afternoon downpours, usually lasting a few hours rather than whole days.

- **Temperatures**: 24-33°C, high humidity
- **Island (Koh Rong Samloem)**: the sea is still enjoyable, but check the weather for the ferries

#### **Southern Vietnam — Chau Doc & Mekong, Ho Chi Minh City (13-16 Oct)**


Still the wet season, but the south is the gentlest part of Vietnam at this time of year: mornings are often clear, with the rain packed into short, intense afternoon bursts. The Mekong Delta is at the height of its high-water season — perfect for the flooded forest of Tra Su.

- **Temperatures**: 24-32°C

#### **⚠️ Central Vietnam — Hoi An, Hué, Phong Nha (17-24 Oct)**


**The trickiest stretch weather-wise.** In central Vietnam, October-November is the **peak of the rainy season**: heavy rainfall, a **real risk of flooding** (Hoi An's old town floods almost every year between October and early November), and an active storm/typhoon season. In Phong Nha, high water can cut off access to the boat-reached caves.

- **Temperatures**: 23-30°C, very high humidity
- **To do**: check the forecast day by day, keep your plans flexible, and pack a raincoat and dry bags

#### **Northern Vietnam — Ninh Binh, Lan Ha Bay, Hanoi (25-31 Oct)**


The grand finale, and the best window of all: the north settles into the **heart of the dry autumn**. Clear skies, low humidity, cool and pleasant temperatures, and calm seas for the cruise in Lan Ha Bay. Late October is one of the finest times of year for Hanoi and Halong.

- **Temperatures**: 21-29°C, dry
- **Note**: by late October, most of Ninh Binh's golden rice paddies have already been harvested

### Complete Packing List (31 days for 4 people)

#### **Clothing (Per Person)**

**TOPS (16-18 pieces total)**
- **8-10 t-shirts/vests** in breathable technical fabric (quick-drying)
- **3 light long-sleeved shirts** for temples, air conditioning, evenings
- **2 "smart" shirts** for restaurants/nights out
- **1 light sweatshirt** for flights and night buses
- **2 swimsuits/bikinis** (absolute minimum - you'll want more!)

**BOTTOMS (10-12 pieces total)**  
- **6 pairs of shorts** light and cool (no heavy jeans)
- **2 pairs of long trousers** breathable, for temples and evenings
- **1 pair of technical trousers** for trekking/activities
- **2-3 light dresses/skirts** for her
- **12+ pairs of underwear** (you sweat a ton!)
- **8-10 pairs of thin socks**

#### **Footwear (Max 4 pairs per person)**

1. **Quality flip-flops** - you'll use them every day
2. **Trekking sandals** like Teva/Keen for hikes and water
3. **Breathable trainers** for cities and transport
4. **Waterproof hiking boots** for anyone doing serious trekking

#### **Essential Accessories**

**For temples and local culture:**
- **2 sarongs/pareos** each (cover shoulders/legs at temples, beach towel, bed sheet)
- **Hat with a visor** - MANDATORY, the sun is brutal
- **Light multi-purpose scarf**

**Protection:**
- **Sunglasses** with a strap
- **SPF 50+ sunscreen** - bring it from Italy, the local stuff often has whitening agents
- **Strong mosquito repellent** (30%+ DEET)
- **Ultralight K-way** or poncho

#### **Electronics and Adapters**


**Great news — you won't need a bag full of different adapters. Vietnam, Laos and Cambodia use almost identical standards (a mix of Type A and C), and Thailand works happily with the same universal adapter too.**

**What you need:**
- **1-2 good-quality universal adapters** with surge protection
- **20,000+ mAh power bank** - essential, phones drain super fast
- **Multiple USB cables** (bring backups!)
- **Small power strip** with a European plug (genius for hotels with a single socket)
- **Waterproof case** for your phone

**Plug types in your countries:**
- **Cambodia**: Type A, C and G plugs; standard voltage 230V at 50Hz
- **Vietnam**: Type A (two flat vertical pins, as in the US/Canada) and Type C (two round pins, as in continental Europe)
- **Thailand**: 220V; sockets generally take round or flat two-pin plugs


#### **Documents and Security**

**Physical documents:**
- Passport (6+ months' validity)
- Printed visas (if required)
- **2 copies** of the passport in separate places
- **Spare passport photos** (for on-the-spot visas)
- International driving permit
- Printed travel insurance

**Digital backups:**
- Photocopies of your documents, kept apart from the originals. Digital copies on your laptop (backed up to Dropbox or another cloud, plus an email to yourself), a photo of your passport on your phone, and a cloud backup of the phone itself
- **Emergency cash** in USD (small denominations for visas and bribes)

### Smart Packing Tips

#### **Backpack vs Suitcase: The Verdict**


**For Southeast Asia, with its many islands and rough terrain, we'd take a backpack over wheeled luggage every time.**

**Ideal setup for 4 people:**
- **2 medium/large backpacks** (40-55L) for the bulk of your luggage and **4 small daypacks** for the valuables. The big bags can go in the aircraft hold and the buses' luggage holds, but the small ones should always stay with you!
- **Osprey Porter 46L** - the perfect backpack, in our experience. It's earned its keep across Europe and Southeast Asia


#### **Ideal Weight and Practicality**

- **Keep your main backpack under 10 kg. You won't need more, and you'll wear far less than you think.**
- **46 litres really is the sweet spot for backpack size. Small enough to carry on board, it lets you check in on your phone, walk past the check-in desk and skip the bag weigh-in altogether.**


#### **Things to Buy THERE Instead of Bringing**

**BUY in Asia (cheaper/better):**
- Local cotton clothes (cool and cheap)
- **Dry bag**: easy to find in Southeast Asia, especially in watery spots like southern Thailand or the Philippines. It's bulky in a bag, so if you won't use it much, buy it there
- Basic toiletries
- **Mosquito repellents**: skip the harsh high-DEET sprays. Ask the locals what they use and buy what they recommend — they know what works best


**BRING from Italy (hard to find or expensive):**
- **Sunscreen**: in parts of Southeast Asia, sunscreen without whitening agents can be hard to track down — and when you do find it, it costs as much as it does back home
- Specific medications
- Spare contact lenses/glasses
- Quality feminine products

#### **Magic Organisation: Packing Cubes**


**If I could give you a single Southeast Asia packing tip, it's this: use packing cubes — and make them compressible ones.**

**Recommended setup per person:**
- 1 cube for underwear/socks
- 1 cube for tops/shirts  
- 1 cube for trousers/shorts
- 1 small cube for electronics
- 
**A dry bag can double as a laundry bag**


#### **Insider Tricks for Night Buses and Boats**

- **Max weight 15kg** per person for comfort on transport
- **Backpack with a rain cover** essential
- 
**A light, waterproof, packable jacket (a €20 one from Decathlon does the job) if you're travelling in the wet-season months. This one garment has saved me and my backpack from sudden tropical storms more times than I can count — including stepping off a minivan at the 4000 Islands in Laos in pouring rain. It also shields your electronics on travel days.**
- **Documents in a waterproof case** always with you
- **Emergency food** (bars) for long journeys

**Remember:** 
**Travelling Southeast Asia in the low season is no great hardship, as long as you know what to expect and plan well.** Your dates are perfect for dodging the crowds and enjoying lower prices, with nature at its post-monsoon best! 

The golden rule? **"When you pack to travel, lay out all your clothes and all your money. Then take half the clothes and twice the money."** Fine, the money half may not be realistic — but the clothes half definitely is!
