## Chau Doc & Mekong — Overview

Welcome to the beating heart of the Mekong Delta, where rivers weave together like silver veins beneath endless skies and the sun sets behind sugar palms swaying in the wind.
Chau Doc is a border town near Cambodia with an atmosphere all its own — a crossroads of cultures where Vietnamese, Chinese (Hoa), Cham and Khmer live side by side, in the town and the countryside around it, each keeping its own traditions alive in faces, dress and architecture. This is the land where sacred Sam Mountain rises from the endless rice paddies like an island of spirituality — the spiritual heart of the Mekong Delta, drawing pilgrims from all over Vietnam.

![The Mekong Delta](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Can_Tho%2C_Vietnam%2C_Floating_Market%2C_Rawing.jpg/1280px-Can_Tho%2C_Vietnam%2C_Floating_Market%2C_Rawing.jpg)

It is also one of the best places in the region to see a more local, less commercial side of the Delta. Here you can glide through the Tra Su Cajuput Forest, a beautiful flooded woodland laced with green waterways, and lose yourself in the floating villages where life still flows to the age-old rhythm of the great river.

### History & Culture

The story of Chau Doc is a fascinating tale of cultural fusion rooted in ancient Cambodia.
The area once belonged to the various kingdoms that dominated the Mekong Delta — Funan, Chenla and the Khmer Empire. It became Vietnamese about 300 years ago, when Mac Cuu, a Chinese general, began pushing Vietnamese and Chinese settlements deeper into Khmer territory.

![Sam Mountain in Chau Doc](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a1/Mountain_Nui_Sam%2C_Chau_Doc.jpg)

In 1815, King Gia Long (Nguyen Anh) of the Nguyen dynasty ordered the Chau Doc Fort built on the western bank of the river. A Chinese mandarin named Diep Hoi was put in charge of the town, and to encourage people to settle and work here, immigrants were offered loans to reclaim land or start businesses across these wild, marshy flatlands.

But it is today's cultural richness that makes Chau Doc truly special.
The Cham around Chau Doc practice a rustic form of Sunni Islam, most likely introduced by seafaring Muslim traders in the late 10th and early 11th centuries and reinforced by the arrival of Muslim Cham from Cambodia into the Mekong Delta in the mid-19th century. So close to the Cambodian border, Chau Doc is home to several ethnic and religious communities where Cambodian Buddhists, Vietnamese Buddhists, ethnic Chinese and Muslim Cham live peacefully side by side.

A fascinating fact:
Children here study Vietnamese at school and Arabic in the evening at the local mosque, built with the help of Muslim brothers from overseas. The community keeps to itself when it comes to marriage — anyone wishing to marry in must convert to Islam — yet mixes freely with its Vietnamese and Khmer neighbours.

### When to Go

October is one of the best times to visit the Mekong Delta!
The flood season, from September to December, is when the Delta is at its best, with a pleasant climate and very little rain.

**What weather to expect in mid-October:**
- **Temperatures**:
Days in Chau Doc are very hot, around 33°C; after nightfall the temperature generally drops to 25°C

- **Rainfall**:
October in Chau Doc can be quite wet — around 300 mm of rain across 18 rainy days — but the summer monsoon gradually eases off in the south, hinting at the return of summer

- **General conditions**:
Between September and November the birds are most active in the early morning and late afternoon, when the forest floods
 - perfect for your visit to the Tra Su Forest!

**The upside**: it is the perfect season to see the lotus flowers in full bloom and the birdlife in the flooded forest.

### Orientation

Chau Doc is a compact, easy-to-navigate town strung along the banks of the Hau Giang River (the Bassac). It sits between this branch of the Mekong and the man-made Vinh Te canal.

**How it's laid out:**

- **Town center**: Along the riverfront, where you'll find the local market, hotels and restaurants. This is where the Vietnamese and Chinese community mostly lives

- **River area**: The Cham live along the Hau River — here you'll find the floating villages and traditional stilt houses
- **Border area**: Home to the Khmer, on the edge of Chau Doc near Cambodia

- **Sam Mountain**: 6 km from Chau Doc, with a sweeping view over the flat plains that ring the mountain

**Mental map to get your bearings:**
- The river flows from northwest to southeast
- The town center is on the eastern bank
- The Cham villages are reachable by boat across the river
- Sam Mountain is west of town (6 km)
- The Tra Su Forest is to the south (30 km)
- The Cambodian border is very close to the southwest

The beauty of Chau Doc lies in its human scale: everything is reachable on foot, by bicycle or with a short moto-taxi ride, and the river is always your reference point for finding your way.
