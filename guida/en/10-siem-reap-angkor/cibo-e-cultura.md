## FOOD & CULTURE - Siem Reap & Angkor

### Where and What to Eat in Siem Reap & Angkor

![Street food in Siem Reap](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Siem_Reap_street_food_%2815932997329%29.jpg/1280px-Siem_Reap_street_food_%2815932997329%29.jpg)

Siem Reap is not only the gateway to the temples of Angkor Wat but also a food-lover's paradise that will win you over to Khmer cuisine. 
This is a tourist hub with a huge dining scene, from traditional Cambodian dishes to cooking from all over the world. Some restaurants do tone down the local flavours to suit what they imagine the Western palate wants — but we'll point you to the real thing.

### Must-Try Typical Dishes

**Fish Amok (អាម៉ុកត្រី) - The National Dish**

Fish Amok, or amok trei, is a steamed Khmer fish curry with a mousse-like texture, and one of Cambodia's national dishes. "Amok" means "steamed" and "trei" means fish. The fish is usually goby, snakehead or catfish — all freshwater species from Cambodia's lakes.

The fish is steamed with curry inside banana leaves until it turns soft and mousse-like. Unlike most Asian curries, it comes out fragrant rather than fiery, thanks to the blend of coconut milk and kroeung.

**Price**: $8-15 in restaurants, $3-5 at street food spots

**Lok Lak (លក់លក់) - The Dancing Beef**

Lok Lak is a savoury Cambodian dish you simply have to try. At first glance it's just a plate of pan-fried beef, but the secret of this Siem Reap speciality is all in the marinade. The beef is left overnight in a mix of fish sauce, lemon, oyster sauce and pepper, which gives Lok Lak its distinctive flavour. It usually arrives on a bed of lettuce, tomato, red onion and cucumber, with rice, a brown sauce and a fried egg.

**Price**: $6-10 in restaurants, $3-5 at street stalls

**Bai Sach Chrouk - The Breakfast of Champions**

Bai Sach Chrouk is sold at street stalls and local markets across central Siem Reap for less than $1. This simple but hugely popular Cambodian breakfast is juicy slices of pork with scrambled eggs and rice.

**Price**: Less than $1

**Khmer Red Curry**

For all its vivid colour, Khmer red curry contains no chilli, which makes it far milder than the usual Thai and Indian curries — perfect if you can't take the heat. The coconut-milk base is built on kroeung, a Khmer curry paste of lemongrass, galangal, kaffir lime zest and turmeric. It's traditionally served with a French baguette.

**Price**: $5-8 in restaurants

### Our Recommended Restaurants and Street Food

**1. The Sugar Palm** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

An outstanding Cambodian restaurant that has well and truly made its name. It bills itself as homestyle Cambodian comfort food, though the menu naturally runs to the usual favourites like Fish Amok and Beef Lok Lak too — and every dish is clean and beautifully done. It's a must whenever I'm in Siem Reap. I always go for the Prahok Ktiss, and I ask them not to hold back on the chillies. Prices sit a little above the average Cambodian restaurant here, and they've crept up further since Gordon Ramsay dropped by and gave the place his blessing. Even so, they remain very reasonable for cooking of this standard.

**Price range**: Starters ~$4.50, Main courses ~$8
**Where**: Taphul Village, Old Market Area

**2. Malis Restaurant** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

The sister of the renowned Malis in Phnom Penh, both owned by the rather elegant Thalias Hospitality Group. The menu is undeniably pricey by Cambodian standards, but the food, the service and the exclusive setting more than justify it. You dine in a lush green garden without ever leaving the city centre, and the local dishes are cooked without compromise, true to traditional recipes and flavours. I'd single out the roast duck with lemongrass — juicy and tender. Come for breakfast, too: they serve all the classic Khmer morning dishes, from Bai Sach Chrouk to Kuy Teav and Lort Char. One of Siem Reap's essential tables.

**Price range**: Starters ~$13, Main courses ~$15-50
**Where**: 6th Street (Sivatha Boulevard)

**3. Cuisine Wat Damnak** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

Named one of the best restaurants in Asia, Cuisine Wat Damnak belongs on any Siem Reap shortlist. Chef Joannès Rivière and his team serve refined Cambodian dishes that marry local flavours with French technique. Tables are hard to come by — the place is famous and opens only for dinner, Tuesday to Saturday — so book as far ahead as you can, and don't leave it until you arrive in Siem Reap. We missed out ourselves: it was fully booked.

**Price range**: Tasting menu ~$60-80
**Where**: Wat Damnak Village

**4. Jomno Restaurant** ⭐⭐⭐⭐

For lunch on our second day we headed to Jomno, one of Siem Reap's top-rated restaurants online. We worked through a spread of local dishes with different proteins: Battambang-style sausage with banana-flower salad, grilled chicken with a bright-yellow korko stew, and river-prawn larb (a minced salad).

**Price range**: Main courses $6-12
**Where**: Siem Reap City Center

**5. Spoons Cafe** ⭐⭐⭐⭐

Spoons serves delicious Cambodian food in a casual, easy-going setting. There's no air conditioning, but we were perfectly comfortable. It's also a training kitchen, where disadvantaged young adults learn cooking and hospitality skills. Don't miss the nime chow (Cambodian spring rolls) — Spoons Cambodia is a social enterprise helping students work their way out of poverty through the trade.

**Price range**: Main courses $4-8
**Where**: Near Old Market

**6. Khmer Grill** ⭐⭐⭐⭐

Ask anyone in Siem Reap where to eat authentic Khmer food and Khmer Grill tops the list. It sits just across the river from the Old Market — an easy walk, or a quick tuk-tuk ride if you're not staying in the centre.

**Price range**: Main courses $5-10
**Where**: Watdamnak Road, Wat Damnak Village

**7. Street Food along the river**

In the afternoon and evening, once the worst of the heat has lifted, excellent street food appears along the river near the Old Market. Whether you're heading into the market to browse or winding down after a night out, it's a lovely spot to grab a bite and stroll along the water.

**Price range**: $1-3 per dish

**8. Pub Street Food Stalls**

In the heart of Siem Reap, Pub Street (Street 8) becomes the centre of the nightlife at sunset. Come dark, it turns pedestrian-only and hums with energy: you can eat everything from Khmer classics to Western favourites, not to mention 75-cent draught beers and crunchy insect snacks.

**Price range**: Beer $0.75, Street food $1-3

### Markets

**Old Market (Psar Chas)** ⭐⭐⭐⭐

Also known as Phsar Chas, the Old Market is a vibrant place to dive into local food culture. Its stalls turn out traditional Cambodian dishes like fish amok, beef lok lak and num banh chok (a rice-noodle dish), and there's plenty of tropical fruit and refreshing drinks to keep you going as you explore.

**Best hours**: 6:00-18:00 (liveliest in the morning)
**Where**: Old Market Area, near Pub Street

**Angkor Night Market** ⭐⭐⭐⭐

The Angkor Night Market is a must for food lovers. Right in the heart of the city, it's a paradise for street-food fans, with a wide spread of local dishes — Khmer noodles, grilled meats, fresh seafood — alongside a good range of international cooking, so there's always something new to try. The lively atmosphere and low prices make it a favourite with locals and tourists alike.

**Hours**: 17:00-24:00 daily
**Where**: Angkor Night Market Road

**Made in Cambodia Market**

Held every Saturday, Sunday and Tuesday in front of the Shinta Mani Resort Siem Reap, this lively open-air market is pricier than most in town — but the originality and quality of the clothes, cosmetics, jewellery, paintings and toys are worth it. Don't miss the Sombai rice spirits, infused with cinnamon, mango, star anise and red chilli.

**Hours**: Saturday, Sunday and Tuesday
**Where**: Street 27, Achar Sva St

### Etiquette and Culture

**In the Temples**

Always dress modestly, with shoulders and knees covered — this goes for men and women alike. It's worth carrying a scarf or sarong to cover up if you're wearing something more revealing. By custom, you remove shoes and hats before entering a pagoda or someone's home. Never turn your back on an image of the Buddha or strike a disrespectful pose in front of one, don't touch the sacred statues, and keep quiet and respectful throughout.

**With Monks**

Monks are held in great respect. Women should avoid touching them or handing anything to them directly: to give a monk something, place it on a cloth or ask a man to pass it on. Touching a monk or novice is considered rude, and strictly taboo for a woman — so take care not to brush against a monk's robes on the street, in a temple or when sharing a tuk-tuk. Rather than hand anything to a monk directly, pass it through a man.

**At the Table**

Always wait for the eldest person to start eating before you do. Most Cambodian meals are eaten with a spoon and fork, with chopsticks for noodle dishes, and food is usually served family-style. Take sensible portions rather than piling up your plate, and try to finish what you take, as wasting food is seen as disrespectful. Cambodians regard the left hand as "impure" and reserved for hygiene, so whenever you eat without utensils — at the Angkor Wat hotels or anywhere else — use your right hand.

**General Behavior**

Don't give or receive objects with the left hand, which is considered impure — use the right hand, or both. Keep your voice down, as loud conversations can come across as rude. Rather than point at people or things, gesture with an open hand or a slight nod of the head. When you offer or receive anything — a gift, money, even a business card — do so with both hands: the gesture signals respect and sincerity.

**Greetings**

The customary way to greet someone in Cambodia is the Sampeah: press your palms together as if praying, in front of your chest, and bow slightly. The higher you raise your hands, the more respect you show — especially to elders or people in authority.

**Common Mistakes to Avoid**
- 
Don't point your feet at people or religious objects — it's considered rude in Cambodian culture. When sitting, tuck your feet under you or angle them away from others

- 
Showing strong emotion is frowned upon. Keep anger, impatience or frustration in check, as they cause a loss of face

- 
Avoid prolonged eye contact

### Useful Words

**Basic Greetings**
- 
Hello: Sous-dey (suh-SDEY)
 - the standard greeting you'll hear all over Cambodia
- 
Formal hello: Chumreap suor (chum-reap SUOR)

- 
Thank you: Arkoun (ar-KOON)

- 
Please: Saum (SOOM)

**At the Table**
- 
Delicious: Ch'nganh (ch-NANG)
 - Cambodians are proud of their cuisine!
- 
Wine: Sra (SRAH), Beer: Sra biər (srah BEE-uh), Coffee: Kafe (kah-FAY), Water, please: Saom Tuk Muoy (sa-ohm took mu-oy)

- 
The bill, please: Saom kitloy (sa-ohm kit-LOY)

- 
Cheers!: Choul mouy (choul MOO-ee)

**Shopping and Numbers**
- 
How much is it?: Boun Maan (boon MAAN)

- 
Expensive!: Thlai (TLAI)
 - useful for haggling in the markets!
- 
I'll take it!: Knhom yok! (khnyom yawk!)

**Responses**
- 
Yes: Baat (BAAT) for men, Jaa (JAH) for women - one of the few words that changes by gender!, No: Adtai (or-TAY), No thank you: Adtai-Arkoun

**Emergencies**
- 
Help!: Chuoy phong! (chhoy pe-ong!)

- 
Hospital: Montipeth (mon-tee-PET)

The best part? 
Khmer words take a bit of practice, especially for English speakers, but don't be shy about trying. Cambodians genuinely appreciate the effort and will happily help you out — even if they share a gentle chuckle with you along the way.

Cambodian food will win you over with its delicate yet complex flavours, and the warmth of the Khmer people will make every meal one to remember. Bon appétit — or, as they say here, "Nyam bai!" (Enjoy your meal!)
