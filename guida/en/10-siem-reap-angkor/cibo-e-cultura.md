## FOOD & CULTURE - Siem Reap & Angkor

### Where and What to Eat in Siem Reap & Angkor

![Street food in Siem Reap](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Siem_Reap_street_food_%2815932997329%29.jpg/1280px-Siem_Reap_street_food_%2815932997329%29.jpg)

Siem Reap is not only the gateway to the temples of Angkor Wat, but also a culinary paradise that will make you fall in love with Khmer cuisine. 
The city is a tourist hub with a vast dining scene, from traditional Cambodian dishes to international cuisines. However, some Siem Reap restaurants tone down the local flavors to suit the presumed Western palate
, but we'll tell you where to find the authentic flavors!

### Must-Try Typical Dishes

**Fish Amok (អាម៉ុកត្រី) - The National Dish**

Fish Amok, or amok trei, is a steamed Khmer fish curry with a mousse-like consistency, considered one of Cambodia's national dishes
. 
The word "amok" means "steamed" and "trei" refers to fish. The fish usually used is goby, snakehead or catfish, all freshwater fish that can be found in Cambodia's lakes
. 

Fish amok is freshwater fish steamed with curry and banana leaves, resulting in a soft, mousse-like texture. Unlike most Asian curries, it gives off a fragrant rather than spicy flavor thanks to the combination of coconut milk and kroeung
.

**Price**: $8-15 in restaurants, $3-5 at street food spots

**Lok Lak (លក់លក់) - The Dancing Beef**

Lok Lak is a savory Cambodian dish that you absolutely must try. On the surface it's simply a plate of pan-fried beef, but the secret of this Siem Reap specialty lies in the marinade
. 
The beef is marinated overnight in a mixture of fish sauce, lemon, oyster sauce and pepper, which gives Lok Lak its unique flavor. You'll probably see Lok Lak served alongside a bed of lettuce, tomato, red onion, cucumber, rice, brown sauce and a fried egg
.

**Price**: $6-10 in restaurants, $3-5 at street stalls

**Bai Sach Chrouk - The Breakfast of Champions**

Bai Sach Chrouk is sold by street food stalls and local markets in central Siem Reap for less than $1. This simple but popular Cambodian breakfast consists of juicy slices of pork, scrambled eggs and rice
.

**Price**: Less than $1

**Khmer Red Curry**

Khmer red curry, despite its vivid color, contains no chili, making it a much milder version of typical Thai and Indian curries. Ideal for those who can't handle spicy food, this coconut-milk-based dish uses kroeung, a Khmer curry paste made with lemongrass, galangal, kaffir lime zest and turmeric. Traditionally served with a French baguette
.

**Price**: $5-8 in restaurants

### Our Recommended Restaurants and Street Food

**1. The Sugar Palm** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

An outstanding Cambodian restaurant that has made a name for itself. They claim to serve homestyle Cambodian comfort food, but of course the menu also includes the usual popular suspects like Fish Amok and Beef Lok Lak. Nevertheless, the dishes are all clean and high quality. Every time I'm in Siem Reap, the Sugar Palm restaurant is a must-visit. Personally, I always go for the Prahok Ktiss every time. Ask them not to cut back on the chilies. The prices are a bit higher than the usual Cambodian restaurant in Siem Reap, and they've risen even more since Gordon Ramsey visited and endorsed the restaurant. Even so, the price level is still very acceptable for a restaurant of this standard
.

**Price range**: Starters ~$4.50, Main courses ~$8
**Where**: Taphul Village, Old Market Area

**2. Malis Restaurant** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

The sister restaurant of the renowned Malis in Phnom Penh, both owned by the rather elegant Thalias Hospitality Group. By Cambodian standards the menu is certainly pricey, but the quality of the food, the service and the exclusive setting are indisputable. Enjoy an evening of dining in a lush green garden without ever having to leave the city center. The local dishes are prepared without compromise according to traditional recipes and original flavors. I specifically recommend the roast duck with lemongrass, juicy and tender. Also, you should really try Malis for breakfast. They serve all the traditional and delicious Khmer breakfasts like Bai Sach Chrouk, Kuy Teav, Lort Char and more. Definitely one of the must-try restaurants in Siem Reap
.

**Price range**: Starters ~$13, Main courses ~$15-50
**Where**: 6th Street (Sivatha Boulevard)

**3. Cuisine Wat Damnak** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

Named one of the best restaurants in Asia, Cuisine Wat Damnak should be on your list of must-try restaurants in Siem Reap. Chef Joannès Rivière and his team serve elevated Cambodian dishes using Cambodian flavors and French culinary techniques. Reservations can be hard to get since this restaurant is very famous in Siem Reap and they open only for dinner from Tuesday to Saturday. Therefore, you should try to book as early as possible. Don't wait until you get to Siem Reap to make reservations. In fact, we weren't able to dine here since the restaurant was fully booked
.

**Price range**: Tasting menu ~$60-80
**Where**: Wat Damnak Village

**4. Jomno Restaurant** ⭐⭐⭐⭐

For lunch on our second day in the city, we headed to Jomno, one of the top-rated restaurants in Siem Reap according to online sources. We tried some local dishes with different proteins: from the Battambang-style sausage served with banana flower salad to grilled chicken with bright yellow korko stew and river prawn larb (minced salad)
.

**Price range**: Main courses $6-12
**Where**: Siem Reap City Center

**5. Spoons Cafe** ⭐⭐⭐⭐

Spoons cafe serves delicious Cambodian cuisine in a casual, relaxing setting. There's no air conditioning but we felt fine. Spoons is also a training facility where disadvantaged young adults are trained in cooking and hospitality service
. 
And they dine on nime chow (Cambodian spring rolls) from Spoons Cambodia, a social enterprise that trains students to escape poverty through hospitality skills
.

**Price range**: Main courses $4-8
**Where**: Near Old Market

**6. Khmer Grill** ⭐⭐⭐⭐

Ask anyone in Siem Reap where to try authentic Khmer food and Khmer Grill will be at the top of their list. It's right across the river from the Old Market and is an easy walk or a quick tuk-tuk ride if you're not staying in the city center area
.

**Price range**: Main courses $5-10
**Where**: Watdamnak Road, Wat Damnak Village

**7. Street Food along the river**

In the afternoon and evening, once the hottest part of the day has passed, you'll find excellent street food along the river near the old market. Before entering the market to explore or, after a night out, this is a great place to grab a snack or a meal and enjoy a stroll along the river
.

**Price range**: $1-3 per dish

**8. Pub Street Food Stalls**

Located in the heart of Siem Reap, Pub Street, or Street 8, becomes the center of nightlife at sunset. At night, the street transforms into a pedestrian paradise, buzzing with energy, where you can savor everything from Khmer cuisine to Western favorites, not to mention the unique experience of 75-cent draft beers and crunchy insect snacks
.

**Price range**: Beer $0.75, Street food $1-3

### Markets

**Old Market (Psar Chas)** ⭐⭐⭐⭐

Also known as Phsar Chas, the Old Market is a vibrant hub of activity where you can immerse yourself in the local food culture. The market is full of food stalls offering traditional Cambodian dishes like fish amok, beef lok lak and num banh chok (a rice noodle dish). You can also find a variety of tropical fruits and refreshing drinks to quench your thirst while exploring the market
.

**Best hours**: 6:00-18:00 (liveliest in the morning)
**Where**: Old Market Area, near Pub Street

**Angkor Night Market** ⭐⭐⭐⭐

The Angkor Night Market is a must-visit for food lovers. Located in the heart of the city, this market is a paradise for street food enthusiasts. You can find a wide variety of local dishes like Khmer noodles, grilled meats and fresh seafood. The market also offers a range of international cuisines, making it a perfect place to try different flavors. The lively atmosphere and affordable prices make it a favorite among both locals and tourists
.

**Hours**: 17:00-24:00 daily
**Where**: Angkor Night Market Road

**Made in Cambodia Market**

Held every Saturday, Sunday and Tuesday, this lively open-air market takes place in front of the Shinta Mani Resort Siem Reap. The goods sold at the Made in Cambodia Market are more expensive than at most Siem Reap markets, but the uniqueness and quality of the clothes, cosmetics, jewelry, paintings and toys are worth the cost. Highly recommended are the Sombai rice spirits, infused with cinnamon, mango, star anise and red chilies
.

**Hours**: Saturday, Sunday and Tuesday
**Where**: Street 27, Achar Sva St

### Etiquette and Culture

**In the Temples**

Always dress modestly. Shoulders and knees must be covered. This applies to both men and women. Consider bringing a scarf or sarong to cover up if you're wearing less conservative clothing. It is customary to remove shoes and hats before entering a pagoda or someone's home
. 
Never turn your back on an image of the Buddha or pose disrespectfully in front of one. Do not touch the sacred statues. Maintain a quiet and respectful demeanor
.

**With Monks**

Monks are highly revered. Women should avoid touching monks or handing objects directly to them. If a woman needs to give something to a monk, she should place it on a cloth or ask a man to pass it to him
. 
Touching a monk or novice is considered rude and is completely taboo if you are a woman. Women should also be careful not to accidentally bump into a monk's robes on the street, in a temple or when sharing a tuk-tuk. Women should not hand anything directly to a monk, but should pass the object to a male intermediary
.

**At the Table**

Always wait for the oldest person to start eating before you begin. Most Cambodian meals are eaten with a spoon and fork. Chopsticks are common for noodle dishes. Food is often served family-style. It is polite to take reasonable portions and not to overfill your plate. Try to finish what's on your plate, since wasting food is considered disrespectful
. 
Cambodians consider the left hand "impure" and suitable only for hygiene purposes, so you should eat with the right hand when not using utensils when dining at the Angkor Wat hotels or other places
.

**General Behavior**

Avoid using the left hand to give or receive objects, as it is considered impure. Use the right hand or both hands. Speak in a moderate tone. Loud conversations can be seen as rude. Avoid pointing your finger directly at people or objects. Instead, use an open hand or a slight nod of the head
. 
When offering or receiving something—a gift, money, or even a business card—it is polite to do so with both hands. This gesture signifies respect and sincerity
.

**Greetings**

When greeting someone in Cambodia, it is customary to perform the Sampeah gesture. This involves pressing the palms together in a prayer-like position in front of the chest and bowing slightly. The higher you raise your hands, the more respect you show, especially to elders or authority figures
.

**Common Mistakes to Avoid**
- 
Don't point your feet at people or religious objects, as this is considered rude in Cambodian culture. When sitting, keep your feet tucked under you or positioned away from others

- 
Showing emotions is considered negative behavior. Anger, impatience or frustration should be hidden as they would lead to a loss of face

- 
Avoid prolonged eye contact

### Useful Words

**Basic Greetings**
- 
Hello: Sous-dey (suh-SDEY)
 - the standard greeting you'll hear all over Cambodia
- 
Formal hello: Chumreap suor (chum-reap SUOR)

- 
Thank you: Arkoun (ar-KOON)

- 
Please: Saum (SOOM)

**At the Table**
- 
Delicious: Ch'nganh (ch-NANG)
 - Cambodians are proud of their cuisine!
- 
Wine: Sra (SRAH), Beer: Sra biər (srah BEE-uh), Coffee: Kafe (kah-FAY), Water, please: Saom Tuk Muoy (sa-ohm took mu-oy)

- 
The bill, please: Saom kitloy (sa-ohm kit-LOY)

- 
Cheers!: Choul mouy (choul MOO-ee)

**Shopping and Numbers**
- 
How much is it?: Boun Maan (boon MAAN)

- 
Expensive!: Thlai (TLAI)
 - useful for haggling in the markets!
- 
I'll take it!: Knhom yok! (khnyom yawk!)

**Responses**
- 
Yes: Baat (BAAT) for men, Jaa (JAH) for women - one of the few words that changes by gender!, No: Adtai (or-TAY), No thank you: Adtai-Arkoun

**Emergencies**
- 
Help!: Chuoy phong! (chhoy pe-ong!)

- 
Hospital: Montipeth (mon-tee-PET)

The best part? 
Even if pronouncing Khmer words may take a bit of practice, especially for English speakers, don't be afraid to try. Cambodians really appreciate the effort and will often help you, even if they might have a little chuckle with you along the way!

Cambodian food will win you over with its delicate yet complex flavors, and the hospitality of the Khmer people will make every meal an unforgettable experience. Bon appétit, or as they say here: "Nyam bai!" (Enjoy your meal!)
