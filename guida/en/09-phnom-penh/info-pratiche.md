## Practical Info — Phnom Penh

### Getting There

**Bus from the coast (Sihanoukville / Koh Rong Samloem)**


After the beach interlude on Koh Rong Samloem, you catch the ferry back to Sihanoukville and carry on to Phnom Penh by bus. The trip takes about 4–5 hours and costs €8–15 per person.

**Reliable companies:**
- **Giant Ibis** (giantibis.com) - the most reliable and comfortable, modern buses with wifi, regular departures from Sihanoukville
- **Virak Buntham** / **BookMeBus** (bookmebus.com) - a good alternative, also faster minivans

**Practical details:**
- 
The Koh Rong Samloem → Sihanoukville ferry takes about 45 minutes: book the morning run to have the whole afternoon in Phnom Penh

- 
Buses arrive at the stations near the riverfront (Sisowath Quay) or the Central Market — a taxi or tuk-tuk to your hotel runs about $2-3

- 
Bring US dollars in cash: in Cambodia the USD is the de facto currency


### Moving On — Fast boat to Chau Doc (Vietnam)


From Phnom Penh you head down the Mekong to Chau Doc by fast boat — one of the most scenic legs of the whole trip, and the way you cross the Cambodia–Vietnam river border. The journey takes about 5–6 hours (including border formalities) and costs between $30–65 per person.

**Reliable companies:**
- **Hang Chau Tourist** (www.hangchautourist.vn) - 
The main operator on this route; it leaves Phnom Penh's International Floating Port in the morning and reaches Chau Doc in the early afternoon

- **Blue Cruiser** - A reliable alternative

**Practical details:**
- 
Vietnamese visa: check the entry requirements in advance (an e-visa sorted before departure is the safest bet). The formalities are handled at the river border

- 
Boarding: International Floating Port, Sisowath Street, Phnom Penh | Arrival: Sunrise Palace, 03A Le Loi Street, Chau Doc

- 
Time for immigration procedures: 1-2 hours


### Getting Around

**Transport apps (RECOMMENDED):**
- **PassApp** - 
The local app most Cambodians use — cheaper than Grab, with more drivers on the road

- **Grab** - 
Available but with fewer drivers than PassApp


**Indicative 2026 prices:**
- 
From the airport to the center: PassApp/Grab $8-12 | Negotiated tuk-tuk $10-18

- 
Short trips (2-3km): PassApp $2-4 | Tuk-tuk $3-6 | Moto (Grab Bike) $1-2

- 
Tuk-tuk for the whole day: $5-10/hour negotiated


**Traditional tuk-tuks:**
- 
Prices: $1 minimum, short trips $1-1.50, across the city $3, maximum $5 for most destinations

- 
Tip: always agree the price before you get in. Walking away often brings it down


**Practical tips:**
- 
Top up PassApp with $10-20 so it all runs smoothly

- 
Always carry small change; drivers often don't have change

### Connectivity & SIM

**WiFi:**
- 
Free WiFi available in most bars, restaurants and hotels. International airports have reliable WiFi

- 
Warning: public WiFi isn't safe for banking — use a VPN


**Local SIMs:**

Three main operators: Cellcard, Smart Axiata and Metfone. All offer 4G coverage in urban areas


**2026 SIM prices:**
- 
Smart Traveller SIM: $5 (300 minutes + 30GB for 15 days)

- 
Metfone: $1-10 (5GB-100GB, 7-30 days)

- 
General tourist SIMs: $5 for 30GB/15 days up to $10 for 100GB/30 days


**Where to buy:**
- 
Phnom Penh airport, official shops in the city. No passport needed

- 
Phone shops, kiosks, street vendors. Passport needed for registration


**eSIM alternatives:**
- **Airalo**: 
Recommended for first-time visitors, costs $10-15 extra but works immediately on arrival

- **Holafly**: 
Unlimited data from $6.90, down to $1.55/day for long stays


### Safety

**Emergency numbers:**
- **Police**: 117
- **Ambulance**: 
119 (in Phnom Penh) or contact the hospital directly

- **Fire brigade**: 118
- **Phnom Penh tourist police**: 
023 726 158; 097 778 0002 (limited English)


**Main risks:**

**Pickpocketing and theft** (HIGH RISK)
- 
Phnom Penh has a high risk of pickpocketing, especially near the river. Watch out for bag snatches from moving scooters. Keep bags in front of you, closed and worn across your chest

- 
Ride in tuk-tuks fitted with metal grilles, which guard against snatch-and-grab thefts


**Common scams:**
- **"Poker scam"**: 
Seemingly friendly people invite tourists to private homes to play cards, where they lose large sums or are forced to withdraw money at gunpoint

- 
Counterfeit bills given as change in shops and venues

- 
Spiked drinks in some bars frequented by foreigners


**Safety recommendations:**
- 
Don't walk alone after dark, avoid dark areas. Don't display phones or cash

- 
Never leave your passport as security for motorbike/scooter rentals

- 
Don't sign police documents you can't read. Always request a report when reporting crimes


**Notes on the police:**
- 
Tourists often struggle to report crimes, the police may not have the resources to help in an emergency, and stations and emergency lines aren't always staffed around the clock


Phnom Penh is generally safe if you take the usual city precautions, but it does call for a little more attention than other Southeast Asian capitals.
