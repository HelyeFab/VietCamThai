# Hanoi — Overview

The first breath you take in Hanoi is steeped in a thousand years of history and in that magical Vietnamese contradiction where motorbikes weave between ancient temples and the scent of phở mingles with that of incense.
Hoan Kiem Lake at the centre of the city serves as a focal point for public life and offers culture, beauty and tranquillity
, while all around unspools the labyrinth of the Old Quarter, where every street tells a thousand years of trade and craftsmanship.

![Hanoi's Old Quarter](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Old_Quarter_street_scene%2C_Hanoi_%281%29_%2838464672752%29.jpg/1280px-Old_Quarter_street_scene%2C_Hanoi_%281%29_%2838464672752%29.jpg)

Hanoi is a captivating capital where history and modernity sit comfortably side by side. Its two most iconic neighbourhoods – the French Quarter and the Old Quarter – offer travellers strikingly different experiences. A short walk apart, each tells its own chapter of Hanoi's rich cultural heritage
. It is a city that makes buying expensive guidebooks pointless, because every corner has a story to whisper and every local has a valuable tip to share.

## History & Culture

![Hoan Kiem Lake and Ngoc Son Temple](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b7/Near_hoan_kiem_lake%2C_hanoi.jpg)

### The Ancient Heart of Hanoi

The Old Quarter (or "36 Streets") originated in the 11th century during the Lý dynasty, when King Lý Thái Tổ moved the capital to Hanoi
.
This ancient commercial district grew up around the 11th century when artisans and merchants organised themselves into guilds, each of which occupied a specific street dedicated to its trade
.

The spirit of that arrangement still lingers today:
the street names still reflect these ancient trades, such as Hàng Bạc (Silver Street) and Hàng Gai (Silk Street), where merchants and artisans have practised their crafts for generations
.
The Old Quarter is characterised by its distinctive "tube houses" – narrow, deep buildings that were designed to minimise taxes on street frontage
.

### The Paris of the East

The French Quarter emerged much later, during French colonial rule over Vietnam (1887-1954). The French demolished parts of the imperial city to build a European-style district with wide boulevards, grand administrative buildings and elegant villas. This area was designed to showcase French power and provide a familiar environment for colonial officials. Today, this colonial legacy is visible in the district's architecture, its urban planning and even in the food culture that blends Vietnamese and French influences
.

![Hanoi Opera House in the French Quarter](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Opera_House_-_Hanoi_-_Vietnam.JPG/1280px-Opera_House_-_Hanoi_-_Vietnam.JPG)

Yellow is traditionally associated with royalty in Vietnamese culture. When the French arrived in Vietnam, they wanted to display their prowess in the Far East and so chose this colour as an expression of power. On a more practical level, yellow is also a colour that absorbs less heat, and if you've ever been to Vietnam, you'll know that it gets extremely hot and humid in summer. Painting the buildings this colour helps keep them cool
.

### The Legend of the Lake of the Returned Sword

According to legend, after defeating Ming China, the emperor Lê Lợi was sailing on the lake when a Golden Turtle God (Kim Qui) surfaced and asked for his magic sword, Heaven's Will. Lợi concluded that Kim Quy had come to reclaim the sword that its master, a local god, the Dragon King (Long Vương), had given to Lợi to defeat Ming China. Later, the Emperor returned the sword to the turtle after he had finished fighting the Chinese. Emperor Lợi renamed the lake to commemorate this event, from its previous name Lục Thủy, meaning "Green Water"
.

## When to Go

### The Perfect Balance of Late October

By the end of October, Hanoi enters the heart of its autumn: clear skies, drier air and low humidity. This is one of the best times of year to explore the enchanting capital city and enjoy the famous autumn season, accompanied by the fragrance of the milk flowers (hoa sữa) in bloom
.

Late-October temperatures are pleasant, typically between 21°C and 29°C, with mild sunny days and cool nights — no more of September's sultry heat
.
Humidity is low and rain becomes rare: October is one of the driest months of the year in Hanoi, so umbrella and raincoat will mostly stay in your suitcase
.

### October: The Gentle Transition

October is one of the best times to visit northern Vietnam. Clearer skies, cooler weather and autumn colours are hallmarks of October. For cultural depth and city charm, Hanoi is unbeatable at this time of year. Autumn leaves, walkable weather and cosy cafés make it feel romantic and relaxed. Don't miss the Temple of Literature, the Old Quarter food tours and a bit of people-watching by the lake
.

One thing to know?
Autumn (October-November) is high season in northern Vietnam, precisely because of the ideal climate. Late October is therefore not a discount month like the summer low season: with good weather demand is high, so it's worth booking accommodation, tours and cruises a few weeks in advance to secure the best rates
.

## Orientation

### Hanoi's Mental Map

Hanoi is laid out like an urban mandala, with
Hoan Kiem Lake at the heart of the district, serving as the focal point of the city's public life. Hoan Kiem is Hanoi's downtown and commercial centre
.

**Old Quarter (North of the Lake)**:
Its northern half is home to the Old Quarter, with small blocks and alleys, and a traditional Vietnamese atmosphere
.
This quarter was the residential, manufacturing and commercial centre, where each street specialised in a specific type of production or trade. Another common name referring to roughly the same area is the 36 Streets (Vietnamese: Hà Nội 36 phố phường), after the 36 streets or guilds that made up the city's urban area
.

**French Quarter (South of the Lake)**:
The southern half has distinctive French-style villas and wide avenues, and is sometimes called the "French Quarter" in modern travel literature. Some notable buildings in Hoan Kiem's "French Quarter" are the Hanoi Opera House, the Sofitel Legend Metropole Hanoi hotel, the National Museum of Vietnamese History, the Grand Palais and the Tonkin Palace
.

**Ba Đình (West)**:
The present-day city is divided into four main districts. The historic Old and French quarters and the newer districts of Ba Dinh (mainly government, military and offices) and Tay Ho (mainly middle-class and expat residential, and commercial)
.

**Tay Ho (North-West)**: A modern residential area around West Lake, where many expats live and where you'll find
Trấn Quốc Pagoda, the oldest Buddhist temple in Hanoi, located on a small island near the south-eastern shore of Hanoi's West Lake
.

### Navigating the Wonderful Madness

The key to finding your way around Hanoi?
As we learned earlier, to cross a street you simply start crossing. Don't change direction. Don't speed up or slow down. Let the traffic flow around you. It's not something for the faint-hearted
. The traffic flows like water – just stay predictable and everything will settle around you!

**Insider Tip**:
The Hoan Kiem Lake Walking Street area opens from 7:00 PM on Friday to 11:59 PM on Sunday every week. This area stretches across most of the Old Quarter streets around the lake
. It's the perfect time to stroll without motorbikes!
