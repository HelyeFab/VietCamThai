## Ho Chi Minh City — Overview

Don't just call it Saigon: Ho Chi Minh City is a human puzzle where bloody history and unbridled capitalism dance together on the pavements. Modern skyscrapers and French colonial architecture blend into a skyline all its own, where the Bitexco Financial Tower rises beside the Saigon Opera House.

![The skyline of Ho Chi Minh City](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/R%C3%ADo_Saig%C3%B3n%2C_Ciudad_Ho_Chi_Minh%2C_Vietnam%2C_2013-08-14%2C_DD_29.JPG/1280px-R%C3%ADo_Saig%C3%B3n%2C_Ciudad_Ho_Chi_Minh%2C_Vietnam%2C_2013-08-14%2C_DD_29.JPG)

It's the largest city in Vietnam and the country's real economic and cultural engine, pulling in millions of tourists and locals alike. This is no postcard — it's a living organism where the past never hides but constantly reinvents itself. The Indochina War era left its own mark on the city's character and hardened its resilience, yet ancient traditions and spiritual customs still thrive right alongside modern life.

### History & Culture

The city traces its origins to the Kingdom of Cambodia, where it began as a small fishing village called Prey Nokor. Vietnamese refugees took the area and renamed it Saigon in 1698. In 1859 it fell to the French, and in 1862 the Vietnamese emperor Tu Duc ceded it to France. As the capital of Cochinchina, Saigon was reshaped into a major port and a grand metropolis of handsome villas, imposing public buildings and well-paved, tree-lined boulevards.

![The Notre-Dame Cathedral of Saigon](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/d7/Saigon_%E2%80%93_Notre_Dame_Cathedral_%282041896237%29.jpg)

But it's the Vietnam War that truly shaped the soul of this city. From 1954 Saigon was the capital of South Vietnam, and through the Second Indochina War in the 1960s and early '70s it served as the headquarters of American military operations. On April 30, 1975, North Vietnamese forces took Saigon, ending the war and reunifying Vietnam. Soon after, Saigon was officially renamed Ho Chi Minh City in honour of the revolutionary leader.

**Insider cultural tidbit**: The official name is Ho Chi Minh City, but plenty of locals and visitors still call it Saigon. The two names are used interchangeably across the country, and you'll still spot "Saigon" on shop signs all over Vietnam, even in Hanoi. It's not nostalgia — it's cultural identity.

### When to Go

In mid-October (your days: October 14-16) Ho Chi Minh City is still deep in the rainy season, but the South is the most forgiving part of Vietnam at this time of year. Mornings are often clear, and the rain tends to bunch into short, heavy late-afternoon downpours: October is still a wet month (on average around 270-330 mm spread over many days), but the showers are usually brief and rarely write off a whole day.

On your days, temperatures run from 24-25°C at night to 31-32°C in the early afternoon. Heavy humidity blowing in off the sea keeps the air thick, so it feels far hotter than the thermometer says and leaves everything sticky. Stand outside at midday for barely ten minutes and your clothes will be soaked with sweat.

The weather here is theatrical — clear skies one moment, dark clouds massing the next, then sudden downpours that leave the streets under calf-deep water, only for the sun to come straight back out. Trainers soak through easily, so pack sandals too. Bring a light raincoat or an umbrella, and try to schedule your outdoor sightseeing for the mornings, usually the driest part of the day.

### Orientation

Ho Chi Minh City is vast — it sprawls across 24 districts — but only a handful will matter to you. They all fan out around District 1, the city's nerve centre, buzzing with the main shopping areas and entertainment venues. The Saigon River winds through several districts, including Binh Thanh, District 1, District 4 and District 7.

**Essential mental map**:

- **District 1**: 
The economic and cultural heart, bordered by Thu Duc City and Districts 3, 4 and 5. This is where you'll find Ho Chi Minh's landmarks — the Independence Palace, Ben Thanh Market, the Central Post Office and Notre Dame Cathedral

- **District 3**: 
Right next to District 1, but with a gentler charm that offsets its bustling neighbour. Life moves a touch more slowly here, among peaceful parks and lovely churches like Tan Dinh Church

- **District 4**: 
An "emerald island" in the heart of Ho Chi Minh City — small, and thoroughly local. A brilliant spot for street food

- **District 5 (Cho Lon)**: 
Vietnam's Chinatown, known as 'Cho Lon'. Famous for its specialist markets and full-flavoured Chinese cooking, it serves up a wealth of culinary treats alongside rich historical and cultural sites

- **District 7**: 
Home to the modern Phu My Hung neighbourhood, one of the city's fastest-growing, fastest-modernising districts

Tan Son Nhat Airport sits in Tan Binh District, about 7 kilometres north of District 1. A taxi costs around 200,000 VND (8-10 dollars) and takes 20-30 minutes.
