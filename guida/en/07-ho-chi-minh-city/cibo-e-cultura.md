## Where and What to Eat in Ho Chi Minh City

### Must-Try Typical Dishes

**Phở (Noodle Soup)**
Vietnam's national dish that you absolutely must try! 
Pho is typically made with four basic ingredients: a clear broth, rice noodles, meat (usually beef or chicken), and herbs, with the southern broth having a sweeter flavor than its northern counterparts
. 
- **Cost**: 
50,000 VND (about €1.65 or $2.05)

![Ben Thanh Market](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/BEN_THANH_MARKET_SAIGON_VIETNAM_JAN_2012_%286966716295%29.jpg/1280px-BEN_THANH_MARKET_SAIGON_VIETNAM_JAN_2012_%286966716295%29.jpg)

 
- **Where to find it**: From street vendors in the morning at 6:00-10:00

**Bánh Mì (Vietnamese Sandwich)**

Often voted "best sandwich in the world," a recognition we can certainly support
! It's literally Vietnam inside a French roll.
- **What's in it**: 
A crispy Vietnamese baguette filled with savory pâté, pork sausage or grilled meat, pickled vegetables, cucumbers, fresh herbs and chili

- **Cost**: 
20,000 VND (about €0.66)
 for a regular bánh mì
- **Best practice**: 
Pho is mainly eaten in the morning
, while bánh mì are perfect at any hour

**Bánh Tráng Nướng ("Vietnamese Pizza")**

Quick to prepare and easy to eat, perfect as a light snack or a fun stop during a street-food tour, with prices from 15,000-35,000 VND
. 
The crispy rice paper is grilled over coals and topped with egg, scallions, dried shrimp and spicy sauce
.

**Gỏi Cuốn (Fresh Rolls)**

Transparent rice-paper rolls filled with fresh herbs and vegetables, rice vermicelli and shrimp or pork
. Fresh, healthy and refreshing - perfect for the tropical climate!

### Markets

**Ben Thanh Market**
- **Address**: 
Central Ho Chi Minh City (Saigon), Vietnam in District 1

- **Hours**: 
The market operates year-round and opens around 6:00 every day until the official closing time of 18:00. After 18:00, the daytime market transforms into a night market that runs until 22:00

- **What to buy**: 
The market has almost 1,500 stalls with more than 6,000 small businesses selling wholesale and retail items from consumer goods to luxury goods

- **Best hours**: 
Early morning (6:00 – 9:00): cooler weather, fewer crowds, fresh produce and lower prices. Late afternoon (16:00 – 17:30): easier to bargain, less heat and a calm atmosphere

- **Tips**: 
Bargain, bargain, bargain! The vendors expect it. Start at about half the asking price. Bring cash. Small bills make shopping faster and easier

**Night Market (after 18:00)**

The Ben Thanh Night Market is ideal for enjoying street food like bun rieu, goi cuon, banh xeo and che while soaking up the vibrant local energy

### Etiquette and Culture

**Behavior in Temples**
- 
Remove your shoes before entering temple buildings. Speak softly or stay silent in sacred areas. Walk slowly and observe what the locals do. Photography may be allowed, but always ask first or look for signs

- 
Dress modestly. Cover shoulders and knees. Remove your shoes before entering the temple building and leave them outside. Speak softly while inside the temple grounds to maintain a peaceful atmosphere

**Table Behavior**
- 
It's polite to wait for the eldest or the host to start eating before you begin. Mastering these customs shows respect for Vietnamese culture and enhances your dining experience

- 
Chopsticks should not be left standing upright in a bowl, as this resembles incense offerings to the dead

- 
When taking or passing dishes, bowls or utensils, use both hands to show respect. Avoid taking large portions of food all at once; it's better to take small amounts and come back for more if needed

**Greetings and Social Interactions**
- 
Handshakes are common, but they may be gentler and more formal than in the West, especially with women or elders. Men usually shake hands with other men. Women often smile and slightly tilt their heads, especially in formal settings. Young people greet elders with both hands and a slight bow

**Common Mistakes to Avoid**
- 
No public displays of affection: holding hands is fine, but kissing or hugging in public is frowned upon. Avoid loud or aggressive behavior: stay calm and polite, even in stressful situations

- 
In Vietnamese culture, the head is considered the most sacred part of the body. Touching someone's head, even in a friendly way, is seen as very disrespectful, especially for adults or elders

- 
Accept tea graciously (even if you don't want it!). Likewise, if you're offered alcohol, it's considered polite to at least taste it - or drink the first glass, if possible

### Useful Words

**Basic Greetings:**
- **Hello**: 
Xin chào (pronunciation: Zin chow) - your universal "Hello" for everyone

- **Thank you**: 
Cảm ơn (pronunciation: Gam-um)
 / 
sounds like "gahm uhn" or "kahm uhn"

- **Goodbye**: 
Tam Biet (pronunciation: tam byet)

- **Excuse me/I'm sorry**: 
Xin loi (pronunciation: seen loy)

**Useful Phrases for Eating:**
- **How much is it?**: 
Bao nhiêu (tiền)? (pronunciation: bahw ngew tee-uhn...)

- **It's too expensive**: 
Đắt quá. (pronunciation: daht?! kwahh?)

- **Water**: 
nước (pronunciation: neu-uhck?)

- **Beer**: 
Bia (pronunciation: bia)

- **Please**: 
Lam on (pronunciation: lahm uhn)

- **Three**:

**Survival Phrases:**
- **I don't understand**: 
Tôi không hiểu (pronunciation: toy kohng high-oo)

- **Can you help me?**: 
Cho tôi hỏi (means "Excuse me" or "May I ask", and it's a phrase you add before asking a question. It makes you sound more polite and helps get the other person's attention)

**Pro Tip**: 
As a foreigner, with context most locals will be able to understand what you're trying to say. And, since understanding is the most important part of communication, even mispronouncing Vietnamese phrases will undoubtedly help you have an unforgettable experience

**Important note on costs**: Prices are current as of 2025-2026. Street food remains extremely cheap: a full meal often costs less than 3-5 euros per person. Tourist restaurants can cost 10-15 euros per person, while upscale dinner spots can reach 20-30 euros.
