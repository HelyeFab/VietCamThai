# FOOD & CULTURE

## Where and What to Eat in Ho Chi Minh City

![Banh Mi — the Vietnamese sandwich](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/B%C3%A1nh_m%C3%AC.jpg/1280px-B%C3%A1nh_m%C3%AC.jpg)

### Must-Try Typical Dishes

**Phở (Noodle Soup)**
Vietnam's national dish, and an absolute must. Pho comes down to four basic ingredients: a clear broth, rice noodles, meat (usually beef or chicken) and herbs — and in the south the broth runs sweeter than up north.
- **Cost**: 
50,000 VND (about €1.65 or $2.05)

![Ben Thanh Market](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/BEN_THANH_MARKET_SAIGON_VIETNAM_JAN_2012_%286966716295%29.jpg/1280px-BEN_THANH_MARKET_SAIGON_VIETNAM_JAN_2012_%286966716295%29.jpg)

 
- **Where to find it**: From street vendors in the morning at 6:00-10:00

**Bánh Mì (Vietnamese Sandwich)**

Often voted the "best sandwich in the world" — a verdict we're happy to back! It's literally Vietnam inside a French roll.
- **What's in it**: 
A crisp Vietnamese baguette packed with savoury pâté, pork sausage or grilled meat, pickled vegetables, cucumber, fresh herbs and chilli
- **Cost**: 
20,000 VND (about €0.66)
 for a regular bánh mì
- **Best practice**: 
Pho is mostly a morning thing, while bánh mì hits the spot at any hour

**Bánh Tráng Nướng ("Vietnamese Pizza")**

Quick to make and easy to eat, perfect as a light snack or a fun stop on a street-food crawl, with prices from 15,000-35,000 VND. The crisp rice paper is grilled over coals and topped with egg, spring onion, dried shrimp and spicy sauce.

**Gỏi Cuốn (Fresh Rolls)**

Translucent rice-paper rolls filled with fresh herbs and vegetables, rice vermicelli and shrimp or pork. Fresh, light and cooling — perfect for the tropical heat!

### Markets

**Ben Thanh Market**
- **Address**: 
Central Ho Chi Minh City (Saigon), Vietnam in District 1

- **Hours**: 
The market runs year-round, opening around 6:00 every day and officially closing at 18:00. After 18:00, the daytime market gives way to a night market that carries on until 22:00
- **What to buy**: 
The market packs in almost 1,500 stalls and more than 6,000 small businesses selling wholesale and retail, from everyday goods to luxury items
- **Best hours**: 
Early morning (6:00 – 9:00): cooler weather, fewer crowds, fresh produce and lower prices. Late afternoon (16:00 – 17:30): easier to bargain, less heat and a calm atmosphere

- **Tips**: 
Bargain, bargain, bargain! The vendors expect it. Start at about half the asking price. Bring cash. Small bills make shopping faster and easier

**Night Market (after 18:00)**

The Ben Thanh Night Market is the place to graze on street food like bun rieu, goi cuon, banh xeo and che while soaking up the buzz

### Etiquette and Culture

**Behavior in Temples**
- 
Take off your shoes before entering temple buildings. Speak softly, or stay silent, in sacred areas. Walk slowly and take your cue from the locals. Photography may be allowed, but always ask first or look for signs

- 
Dress modestly and cover your shoulders and knees. Take off your shoes before entering the temple building and leave them outside. Keep your voice down within the temple grounds to preserve the calm

**Table Behavior**
- 
It's polite to wait for the eldest person or the host to start eating before you tuck in. Getting these customs right shows respect for Vietnamese culture and makes the meal all the better

- 
Chopsticks should not be left standing upright in a bowl, as this resembles incense offerings to the dead

- 
When taking or passing dishes, bowls or utensils, use both hands as a mark of respect. Don't heap your plate all at once; take small amounts and go back for more if you like

**Greetings and Social Interactions**
- 
Handshakes are common, but often gentler and more formal than in the West, especially with women or elders. Men usually shake hands with other men. Women tend to smile and tilt their heads slightly, particularly in formal settings. Younger people greet their elders with both hands and a slight bow

**Common Mistakes to Avoid**
- 
No public displays of affection: holding hands is fine, but kissing or hugging in public is frowned upon. Steer clear of loud or aggressive behaviour: stay calm and polite, even when things get stressful

- 
In Vietnamese culture, the head is regarded as the most sacred part of the body. Touching someone's head, even affectionately, comes across as deeply disrespectful, especially with adults or elders

- 
Accept tea graciously (even if you don't fancy it!). Likewise, if you're offered alcohol, it's polite to at least taste it — or, if you can, drink the first glass

### Useful Words

**Basic Greetings:**
- **Hello**: 
Xin chào (pronunciation: Zin chow) - your universal "Hello" for everyone

- **Thank you**: 
Cảm ơn (pronunciation: Gam-um)
 / 
sounds like "gahm uhn" or "kahm uhn"

- **Goodbye**: 
Tam Biet (pronunciation: tam byet)

- **Excuse me/I'm sorry**: 
Xin loi (pronunciation: seen loy)

**Useful Phrases for Eating:**
- **How much is it?**: 
Bao nhiêu (tiền)? (pronunciation: bahw ngew tee-uhn...)

- **It's too expensive**: 
Đắt quá. (pronunciation: daht?! kwahh?)

- **Water**: 
nước (pronunciation: neu-uhck?)

- **Beer**: 
Bia (pronunciation: bia)

- **Please**: 
Lam on (pronunciation: lahm uhn)

**Basic Numbers:**

mot

- **Two**: 
hai
 
- **Three**: 
ba

- **Four**:

- **Five**:

**Survival Phrases:**
- **I don't understand**: 
Tôi không hiểu (pronunciation: toy kohng high-oo)

- **Can you help me?**: 
Cho tôi hỏi (means "Excuse me" or "May I ask", and it's a phrase you add before asking a question. It makes you sound more polite and helps get the other person's attention)

**Pro Tip**: 
As a foreigner, you'll find that with a bit of context most locals can work out what you're trying to say. And since being understood is what really counts, even mangling a few Vietnamese phrases will go a long way towards an unforgettable trip

**Important note on costs**: Prices are current as of 2025-2026. Street food stays remarkably cheap: a full meal often comes to less than 3-5 euros per person. Tourist restaurants run to 10-15 euros per person, while smarter dinner spots can reach 20-30 euros.
