## Phong Nha — Overview

Picture gliding along an underground river as torchlight plays across thousand-year-old stalactites, while somewhere above you hides the largest cave on Earth — so vast it could swallow a 40-storey skyscraper.
The small village of Phong Nha, in Quang Binh province, sits at the epicentre of one of the most spectacular cave systems on the planet — the place where, in 1990, a local woodcutter named Ho Khanh stumbled on what turned out to be the largest cave passage in the world.

![Entrance of the Phong Nha Cave by boat](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Phong_Nha_cave_entrance.jpg/1280px-Phong_Nha_cave_entrance.jpg)

Phong Nha is more than a destination — it is a journey through geological time. The karst landscape here began to form 400 million years ago, making it the oldest large-scale karst system in Asia. During the Vietnam War, the area was one of the North's main logistics hubs for moving soldiers and supplies to the southern front: stores were stockpiled in the caves, then reloaded onto smaller vehicles for the run south beyond the DMZ. Today the same caves that once hid field hospitals and munitions open onto breathtaking natural wonders.

### History & Culture

**The discovery that changed everything**

![Phong Nha-Ke Bang National Park](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Ban_do_Phong_Nha-Ke_Bang.jpg/1280px-Ban_do_Phong_Nha-Ke_Bang.jpg)

Ho Khanh was born in 1969 in Son Trach (now Phong Nha), at a time when life here was desperately hard: farming was primitive, there was no electricity, schools and clinics were few, and families got by growing what they could and foraging in the surrounding jungle. In the 1980s, soaring demand for agarwood sent the people of Phong Nha on a frantic hunt through the limestone mountains along the Vietnam-Laos border, selling this rare and costly jungle tree to traders who exported it to China and the Middle East.

He came across the cave entrance by chance in 1990, on one of those routine trips into the forest in search of timber. Caught in a heavy thunderstorm, he sheltered against a cliff to wait out the downpour — and at its base found an opening from which a powerful wind blew and the sound of a distant river drifted up. Caves meant little to anyone back then, though, so he thought no more of it.

**The awakening of a legend**

In 2007, Howard Limbert, who led the British-Vietnamese cave-exploration expedition, was going from village to village asking about unusual caves, and Hồ Khanh told him what he had seen. Howard suspected at once that this might be something huge, and urged him to find the entrance again. Hồ Khanh searched for months without luck, until in 2008 he finally relocated the cave and carefully marked its position.

In 2009, Hang Son Doong was officially surveyed and measured by the British-Vietnamese Cave Expedition Team, again led by Howard Limbert. The team went on to declare it the largest natural cave in the world, with a volume of 38.5 million m³ — an announcement made on 14/04/2009.

**War and rebirth**

A vital chapter of Phong Nha's story belongs to the North Vietnamese women who volunteered for the war effort. After the American escalation, North Vietnam rerouted its main supply line through Laos, rebuilding old stretches of the Ho Chi Minh Trail and cutting new ones. On any given day of the war, some 2,000 workers were out building and repairing it. By 1975 there were thousands of kilometres of trail, most of it paved. One stretch, Highway 20, was named after the average age of the engineers and labourers who built it.

During the war, the national park served as a staging area for the North, and the Phong Nha cave became a kind of miniature harbour: boats were hidden inside, and a little deeper in lay a hospital.

### When to Go

**Late October: the high-water season**

Temperatures stay pleasant for most of the year, hottest from June to August and coolest from December to February, with an annual average of around 23°C - 25°C. The rain is heaviest in October and November — and it's that rain that keeps the park's vegetation so lush.

**Your dates (23-24 October) — what to really expect:**

You'll be there at the height of the rainy season: late October is one of the wettest stretches of the year, with frequent, heavy rain. Temperatures ease to around 22°C-28°C, humidity stays high, the sky is often overcast, and sudden downpours are common. The key practical point: with the heavy rains from September to November, the Son River can rise fast, and water caves like the Phong Nha Cave (the one you visit by boat) may have restricted access or close altogether when it floods.

**What to expect:**
- **Daytime temperatures**: 22-28°C with high humidity  
- **Inside the caves**: a constant 20-22°C (bring a light jacket!)
- **Rainfall**: rain likely, possibly prolonged — keep your schedule flexible
- **High water**: river caves may restrict access or close if the river rises; dry caves like Paradise Cave generally remain visitable
- **Trails**: the jungle becomes muddy and slippery, and some adventure tours may be cancelled for safety

The advice is simple: on your days, always check ahead (with your hotel or a local operator) whether the Phong Nha Cave and the river tours are running, and keep a land-based alternative up your sleeve in case the water is high.

### Getting Your Bearings

**How the area is laid out**

Phong Nha lies about 500 km south of Hanoi and 40 km north of Dong Hoi. The town sits at the edge of the park, just off the main highway, and has grown steadily since the caves were discovered.

**A mental map to find your way:**

🏘️ **Phong Nha Town (centre)**: 
Most travellers base themselves in town, along the Son River, where dozens of small homestays and guesthouses line the strip alongside a handful of standard 2-3 star Vietnamese hotels and three backpacker hostels. Phong Nha's only international hotel, Victory Road Villas, is also here, on the river.

🍽️ **The restaurant hub**: 
Most restaurants cluster near the backpacker accommodation, though the dining scene is on the small side. The guesthouse strip runs for almost 2 km, and most of the nicer places to stay are down on the river, away from the main restaurant cluster.

🚤 **The pier area (Cuo Lac)**: 
The dragon boats that drift through the national park set off from Cuo Lac, near the Phong Nha Cave boat station. This part of town has an ATM and restaurants specialising in local food like spit-roast pork and noodles.

🏞️ **The surrounding countryside**: 
Nature lovers are better off booking a boutique homestay, farmstay or villa out in the rice paddies, just beyond town. Here you're steeped in rural life and wake to beautiful views every morning.

🌸 **Bong Lai Valley**: 
A rural pocket about 8 km from Phong Nha town, home to the famous Duck Stop for sunset beers and The Pub with Cold Beer — an all-day watering hole serving fresh chicken. Cycle out here for the real rural Vietnam.
