## Hoi An — Overview

Imagine walking at sunset through cobbled lanes lit by thousands of colourful lanterns reflected in the Thu Bon River, while the air is scented with spices and incense.
Simply wandering its streets and quieter back alleys, especially as the sun goes down and the lanterns flicker on, is an experience in itself
.
Hoi An is an exceptionally well-preserved example of a Southeast Asian trading port dating from the 15th to the 19th century
.

![Hoi An lit up by lanterns](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/H%E1%BB%99i_An%2C_Ancient_Town%2C_2020-01_CN-05.jpg/1280px-H%E1%BB%99i_An%2C_Ancient_Town%2C_2020-01_CN-05.jpg)

Hoi An is an exceptional manifestation of the fusion of cultures over time in an international trading port. Its buildings and street layout reflect the influences, both indigenous and foreign, that combined to produce this unique heritage site
. It's what happens when Japanese, Chinese, Vietnamese and Europeans live and trade side by side for centuries: an architectural masterpiece that adds up to far more than the sum of its parts.

### History & Culture

Hoi An captivates visitors with a history stretching back more than 2,000 years, when it was an important trading port along the ancient Asian maritime routes
. But its golden age truly begins in the 15th century, when for centuries it became
the centre of trade with the Chinese, Japanese and other peoples, who left it a rich and varied architectural heritage
.

![The Japanese Bridge of Hoi An](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Japanese_covered-bridge_of_Hoi_An_in_2015_07.jpg/1280px-Japanese_covered-bridge_of_Hoi_An_in_2015_07.jpg)

The most fascinating story?
Hai Pho, as Hoi An was originally known, was a town split in two, and it was the Japanese who built the famous "covered bridge" — now the city's emblem — to link their quarter with the Chinese one
. It was never just a bridge: it was a diplomatic gesture in wood and stone.

What's more, Hoi An became one of the gateways for Christianity, with Catholic missionaries who contributed to the creation of Quốc ngữ, the current Vietnamese writing system
. It was right here in Hoi An that Alexandre de Rhodes landed, the inventor of the alphabet that made it possible to read Vietnamese in Latin characters — essentially the man who allowed us Westerners to at least vaguely pronounce the names of the dishes on the menus!

Hoi An's decline as a port was, ironically, its salvation:
this spared the city from the rapid urbanisation of 20th-century Vietnam
.
Hoi An was not caught up in the bloody Vietnam War and so, unlike so many other cities, it was able to preserve its ancient character
.

### When to Go

Let's be honest: your dates (17-19 October) fall right at the start of the rainy season in central Vietnam, which in Hoi An is the wettest time of the year.
From October to November rainfall reaches its peak — October is on average the rainiest month, with around 400-500 mm of rain spread over about twenty days — and the rains can be torrential
. Temperatures nonetheless stay mild and pleasant, between 24°C and 30°C, with very high humidity.

It must be said clearly:
the Old Town, which sits right beside the Thu Bon River, tends to flood precisely in October and early November. Almost every year the riverside streets (Bach Dang, An Hoi) end up underwater for one to three days, and in the worst years the water rises considerably
. It's also peak season for tropical storms and typhoons along the central coast, so a downpour is not the exception but the norm.

What this means for you in practice:
check the forecasts and weather bulletins in the days before your arrival, keep your schedule flexible and pack a rain jacket, shoes that can get wet and a waterproof cover for your backpack
. It's not a sunny-postcard time of year, but it has its own magic: the Old Town wrapped in rain, with the lanterns reflected on the wet tarmac, offers atmospheres and photos that the dry season can't provide. Make the most of the clear spells to head out, and take shelter in the cafés and covered heritage houses when the sky lets loose.

### Getting Your Bearings

Hoi An is one of those cities where getting lost is a pleasure, but finding your way is still useful.
The historic centre, in the Minh An district, covers about 2 square kilometres of narrow, winding streets loosely laid out on a grid
.

**The heart: the Old Town**

The historic centre of Hoi An lies north of the Thu Bon River
. This is where everything is concentrated:
near the riverbank is Bach Dang Street, while Nguyen Thai Hoc and Tran Phu streets connect to Nguyen Thi Minh Khai Street via the Japanese Bridge. Tran Phu Street, historically the main artery from the Japanese Bridge to the Chaozhou Community Hall
, is the backbone of the centre.

**South of the river: the authentic Hoi An**

To the south lie residential neighbourhoods where you can discover the Vietnamese way of life
. Here you'll find the local markets, the less touristy craft workshops and the restaurants frequented by locals.

**The beaches: An Bang and around**

An Bang is the most popular beach in the area and lies 5 kilometres north of the city centre
. Easily reached by bicycle or motorbike-taxi, it's perfect for alternating culture and relaxation.

**Mental map to find your way:**
- Japanese Bridge = the centre of the centre, your point of reference
- Tran Phu Street = the main street for tailored shopping
- Bach Dang Street = romantic riverfront for aperitifs
- An Bang = beach 15 minutes by bike to the north
- My Son = Cham sanctuary 1.5 hours to the west

The beauty of it is that
the historic centre is small enough to explore at an easy, unhurried pace
. You can walk from one side to the other in 30 minutes — which means more time for cao lau and white rose, and less time squinting at Google Maps!
